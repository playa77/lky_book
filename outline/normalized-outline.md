## PART I — THE SHOCK *(14,000 words)*

### Chapter 1: The World You Think You Know *(7,000 words)* — style/tone: global

| Unit | Title | Argumentative Function | Claims/Threads | Words |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| S1 | The Landing | Disorient the reader with a concrete, sensory scene of a Westerner encountering a modern Chinese megacity — scale, competence, the visceral gap between expectation and reality. Establishes the book's method: start with concrete experience, not abstraction. | The West's mental model of Asia is obsolete; the physical evidence of Asian development is overwhelming when encountered firsthand; the emotional arc from defensiveness to recalibration is the reader's intended journey. | 1,200 |
| S2 | The Sixty-Year Pivot | Present the raw magnitude of Asia's transformation (1965–2025) through specific comparisons — GDP, life expectancy, literacy, infrastructure — framed not as statistics but as human-scale change. Explain why this isn't common Western knowledge. | Asia's starting conditions were worse than most Westerners imagine; the speed of transformation has no Western precedent; media framing and cultural distance explain the knowledge gap. Plants the book's foundational premise: something worked there. | 1,500 |
| S3 | Singapore: Not a Miracle, a Method | Use Singapore as the purest case study — from malarial swamp to first-world nation in one generation, with no resources and hostile neighbors. Establish the distinction between "miracle" (obscures method) and "method" (the book's subject). Introduce LKY as the architect. | Singapore's outcomes were engineered, not accidental; the method is replicable in principle; LKY is the book's primary diagnostic lens; "miracle" is an evasion. | 1,500 |
| S4 | The Variations | Extend the pattern across South Korea, China, Vietnam, and Malaysia. Extract the common thread: disciplined governance, long time horizons, human capital investment, pragmatism over ideology. | The pattern generalizes beyond Singapore; no single model, but shared principles; the common thread is governance quality, not culture or luck. | 1,800 |
| S5 | The Honest Question | Preempt the "authoritarian apology" charge. Reframe the book's purpose: not admiration tourism but a serious inquiry into why societies that started with less built more. State the organizing question that drives everything that follows. | The book is about outcomes and methods, not endorsement of systems; the West's own tools produced better results when applied seriously elsewhere; the honest question: what does this tell us about the societies that started with more and built less? | 1,000 |
