LKY's central diagnostic insight was simple enough to be stated in a sentence: success contains the seeds of its own reversal, because success produces comfort, comfort produces complacency, and complacency produces fragility. He did not discover this — it is among the oldest observations in political thought — but he applied it to the West with a specificity that made it uncomfortable to dismiss. The mechanism he described was not "decline," as a vague atmospheric condition. It was a chain of causation with identifiable links, each of which could be observed in operation.

The first link is that wealth creates insulation from consequences. A society that has been rich for two or three generations loses the lived memory of scarcity. Decisions that would have been recognized as suicidal by people who remembered hunger, war, or collapse are made by people who have never experienced any of those things and who operate on the unexamined assumption that the baseline of civilized life is self-maintaining. LKY's generation — the generation that fought the Japanese occupation, survived the war, built a country from a swamp — understood viscerally that order is not a default condition. His children's generation, he observed, increasingly did not. What was true in Singapore was, in his view, far more advanced in the West, where prosperity had been accumulating not for decades but for centuries.

The second link is that comfort becomes the electorate's dominant demand. This is not a moral observation; it is a structural one. In a democracy, voters reward politicians who make life easier and punish politicians who make life harder, even when the hard path is the necessary one. The incentive gradient runs entirely in one direction: toward immediate gratification and away from deferred cost. A politician who proposes to raise taxes to fund infrastructure that will pay off in twenty years competes against a politician who proposes to cut taxes and borrow the difference. On election day, the borrowing politician wins reliably. This is not because voters are irrational. It is because the costs of the irresponsible choice arrive on a different timescale than the next election. LKY's observation was that the gap between the electoral cycle and the consequences cycle had become a structural vulnerability — one that mature democracies were structurally incapable of addressing, because the mechanism that would be required to address it was the very mechanism producing it.

The third link is that the habits and institutions that produced prosperity are consumed by it. A society that once rewarded discipline, deferred gratification, and long-term investment gradually comes to reward consumption, immediate satisfaction, and short-term extraction. The family that built a business over thirty years raises children who have never seen anyone build anything, and those children inherit the business, and within a generation or two the business is sold or hollowed out. LKY saw this as the predictable lifecycle of institutions as well as families. He was not describing genetic decline. He was describing incentive decay: once the payoff for discipline is no longer survival but marginal comfort, the discipline itself erodes. There is no force maintaining it other than cultural transmission, and cultural transmission is fragile.

What made LKY's version of this argument distinctive was his refusal to treat it as a philosophical lament. He was not a declinist in the Spenglerian sense, and he had no patience for the genre of Western self-criticism that treats decline as a mood. He was an engineer describing a feedback loop that, left uncorrected, would produce a specific outcome on a specific timeline. "I am not a pessimist," he said in 2013. "I am a realist. I see things as they are, not as I wish them to be." The realism was that the feedback loop had no internal braking mechanism. Every link in the chain — wealth, comfort, complacency, fragility — reinforced the next. Wealth enabled comfort. Comfort rewarded politicians who promised more of it. Complacency prevented voters from noticing that the bill was accumulating. Fragility ensured that when the bill arrived, the society's capacity to absorb it had been degraded by the decades of inattention that preceded it.

The mechanism is worth dwelling on because it is endogenous. This is the most uncomfortable implication of LKY's diagnosis. Western decline, on his account, was not something that happened to the West. It was not imposed by foreign competition, by demographic bad luck, or by the rise of China. Those factors mattered, but they were accelerants, not causes. The cause was the West's own success — more precisely, the West's inability to manage the consequences of its success. No external enemy had forced Western democracies to accumulate unsustainable debt. No foreign power had compelled them to allow infrastructure to decay or education standards to slip or family formation to collapse. Those were choices, or the accumulated consequences of choices, and the traceable responsibility for them lay with the institutions and electorates of the societies experiencing them. This is what made LKY's framing, for all his admiration of the West, fundamentally unflattering. He was telling people who considered themselves the victims of circumstance that they were the authors of their own predicament — and that the deepest expression of that predicament was the refusal to acknowledge authorship.

Historical parallels exist, and LKY was aware of them, though he used them sparingly. The Roman Republic did not collapse because it was weak. It collapsed, in significant part, because its success produced a political economy of bread and circuses — a governing class that purchased short-term stability with resources that should have secured long-term survival. The British Empire did not lose its position because it was conquered. It lost it because the economic discipline that built the empire was steadily eroded by the comfort the empire produced, and by the time the reckoning arrived in the form of two world wars, the accumulated habits of underinvestment could not be reversed in time. These analogies are imprecise, as all historical analogies are, and LKY did not lean on them as proofs. He used them as pattern recognition: the same causal structure, appearing in different contexts, producing the same result. When a mechanism recurs across civilizations, technologies, and centuries, it is worth treating as a mechanism rather than a coincidence.

The trap, in LKY's framing, was that democratic systems had no obvious way out. Authoritarian systems can, in principle, impose discipline from above — though they rarely do so wisely, and their record of long-term governance is worse than democracies' on nearly every measure except the one under discussion. Democracies cannot impose discipline from above, because the people who would impose it are elected by the people who would be disciplined. The mechanism that creates the problem also disables the solution. A democratic society that has entered the comfort trap can only escape it if voters choose to accept costs in the present for benefits in the future — which is exactly the tradeoff the trap makes irrational. LKY did not claim this was impossible. He claimed it was unlikely, and he spent the last two decades of his life cataloguing the accumulating evidence that the unlikely was becoming the unthinkable.

The evidence he tracked was not abstract. It was specific and measurable: debt-to-GDP ratios that had crossed thresholds from which no democratic society had ever returned without crisis; infrastructure maintenance backlogs that represented deferred costs no electoral cycle would ever force into the present; educational outcomes in which per-pupil spending rose while competencies fell; demographic trajectories in which birth rates dropped below replacement without any policy response capable of reversing them. In each case, the pattern was the same: a society consuming resources its successors would need, with no mechanism for forcing the consumption to stop. This is the comfort trap — not a rhetorical construct but a set of observable, measurable dynamics, each of which persists because the people with the power to stop it are the people who benefit from its continuation. The rest of this chapter examines the specific institutional feedback loops that the trap produces, and the question LKY posed that Western societies have yet to answer: whether a democracy that has entered the trap can recognize it in time to escape.
