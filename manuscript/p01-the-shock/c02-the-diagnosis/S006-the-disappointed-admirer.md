In his final years, Lee Kuan Yew gave a series of interviews that read less like the reflections of a retired statesman and more like the discharge notes of a diagnostician who has stopped expecting his patient to follow the treatment plan. He was in his late eighties, speaking with the clarity of someone who isn't running for anything, doesn't need anything, and has concluded that politeness is a poor substitute for accuracy when the stakes are civilizational.

The subject, consistently, was the West — and the assessment was grim. "If you talk about gridlock in Washington," he told one interviewer in 2013, "I do not see it being solved in the next five or ten years, because the two parties are ideologically far apart and their constituencies want them to be so." This was not the complaint of an anti-Western ideologue. It was the diagnosis of a disappointed admirer — someone who had spent his adult life studying Western institutions, borrowing from them, and building with them, and who had concluded that the civilizations that created them were abandoning the disciplines that made them work.

That LKY served not as a Western antagonist but as a Western diagnostician is the most important thing to understand about him, and the reason his critique rewards attention from people who will never agree with his methods. If his position had been the predictable resentment of a postcolonial nationalist — the suspicion of Western motives, the insistence on Asian exceptionalism, the reflexive rejection of anything that smelled of colonialism — his pessimism about the West would be unremarkable. That was the default position in much of the developing world. LKY's position was the opposite. He was an insider making an insider's critique, and insiders making uncomfortable observations about their own institutions are worth more than outsiders making the same observation from resentment.

He understood the West from the inside — literally. He read law at Cambridge in the late 1940s, a colonial subject at the intellectual center of the British Empire, and earned a double first, a distinction awarded to only a handful of candidates across the entire university each year. He was called to the bar at the Middle Temple and practiced law in London before returning to Singapore. The legal system he built in his own country was recognizably British in its architecture: an independent judiciary, the common law tradition, rules of evidence and procedure borrowed directly from London. He governed a multiracial city-state using institutional tools he had mastered among the people whose empire he would help dismantle. His vantage point was unusual: someone who understood Western institutions from within well enough to build a functioning nation-state with them, and who understood them from enough distance to see what their own custodians could not. When he criticized Western governance, he was criticizing a system he had studied, admired, adapted, and — for a long time — expected to prevail.

The arc of that criticism is worth tracing because it did not begin where it ended. In the 1970s, LKY was an open admirer of Western achievement. He called the United States "the most dynamic society in the world" and credited American openness and inventiveness with creating the conditions for postwar prosperity. He positioned Singapore emphatically with the Western alliance during the Cold War, against the Communist insurgency that was active across Southeast Asia. He sent his children to Western universities, hired Western economists to design Singapore's development strategy, and invited American and European multinationals into the country as the engine of its industrialization. His assumption, shared by the generation of Asian leaders who came of age during decolonization, was that the West had figured something out — that the combination of open markets, rule of law, and meritocratic institutions represented the high-water mark of human social organization, and that the task for countries like Singapore was to catch up.

By the mid-1980s and accelerating through the 1990s, the admiration acquired an edge. LKY began speaking publicly about self-inflicted vulnerabilities he was observing: welfare systems that had crossed from safety net to permanent dependency, education systems no longer producing disciplined graduates at scale, political cultures that had become allergic to long-term thinking because democracies rewarded short-term gratification. Some of these observations were delivered in speeches to Western audiences, which made them harder to dismiss. He was not an Asian nationalist making an abstract critique of Western decadence. He was Singapore's prime minister, standing at a podium in London or Washington, telling his hosts that they were not taking their own problems seriously.

The shift was not ideological — it was, in his framing, observational. He watched Western societies accumulate problems their institutional immune systems no longer seemed capable of registering. The mechanisms that had produced prosperity — discipline, deferred gratification, investment in future capacity — were being dismantled by the prosperity they had created. The word "decline" entered his vocabulary without the usual diplomatic softening, because he did not see what would reverse the trajectory he was describing.

By the 2010s, in his final interviews and in *One Man's View of the World*, the tone had moved from concern to something closer to resignation. American governance was "gridlocked." European welfare states were "unsustainable." Western political cultures had become "unable to face hard truths." The patient was not merely sick — it was refusing to acknowledge the diagnosis.

This trajectory is significant not because LKY was uniquely prescient — though on specific questions, from the unsustainability of unfunded entitlements to the consequences of educational decline, the record is uncomfortable — but because it reveals his method. He did not arrive at his conclusions through ideology. He arrived through watching what worked and what stopped working. The same pragmatism that led him to admire Western institutions in the 1970s led him to diagnose their failures forty years later. The standard never changed. The performance did.

This is what makes him a diagnostician rather than a polemicist, and why the chapters that follow take his analysis seriously — not because every LKY position transfers intact to Western democracies, and not because his own record was free of costs, but because a diagnostic method that remains valid when the evidence changes direction is more valuable than a thesis that doesn't survive contact with inconvenient facts. If the West is in decline, the question that matters is not whose fault it is. It's what broke, how it broke, and whether it can be fixed with the tools available.

LKY spent five decades answering that question about every society he studied. The answers were sometimes wrong, often uncomfortable, and always worth confronting. The rest of this book tries, imperfectly, to live up to that standard.
