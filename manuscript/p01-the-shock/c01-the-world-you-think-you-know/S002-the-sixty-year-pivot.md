The tooth is attached to a story. A specific one, repeated so often and for so long that it has stopped feeling like a story and started feeling like the shape of the world. The story goes something like this: the West has developed through a unique combination of institutions, values, and historical accidents — the Enlightenment, the Industrial Revolution, democratic governance, property rights — and the rest of the world is either catching up slowly, failing to catch up at all, or succeeding only by copying Western models under Western supervision. Development is a Western export. Progress has an accent.

The story is not malicious. Most of the people who tell it believe it. But it has one problem, which is that it does not survive contact with the numbers.

In 1965, Singapore's GDP per capita was approximately five hundred dollars — lower than Jamaica's, lower than Mexico's, lower than Argentina's. The island had no natural resources. It had just been expelled from the Malaysian federation against its will. Its drinking water came from a treaty with a larger, hostile neighbor that could abrogate the treaty at any time. Its population of two million was divided by race, language, and religion in proportions that had produced lethal riots the year before. Objectively speaking, Singapore in 1965 was not a country that was supposed to succeed. It was a country that was supposed to be a cautionary tale.

In the same year, South Korea's GDP per capita was below Ghana's. The Korean Peninsula had been reduced to rubble by a war that ended twelve years earlier. The country was governed by a military dictatorship. It had no significant natural resources, no industrial base, and a population that was largely illiterate by modern standards. The consensus among development economists at the time was that South Korea would remain poor, dependent on American aid, and politically unstable for the foreseeable future. This consensus was published, peer-reviewed, and entirely wrong.

China in 1965 was in the middle of the Cultural Revolution's opening convulsions. Its economy was closed, its universities were being dismantled, and its per capita income placed it among the poorest countries on earth. Shenzhen, the city that four decades later would house more skyscrapers than the entire United States built in the twentieth century, was a fishing village of thirty thousand people whose primary economic activities were harvesting oysters and smuggling goods across the border into British Hong Kong.

Vietnam was at war. Its north was being bombed by American aircraft at a rate that would eventually exceed the total tonnage dropped in all of World War II. Its economy did not exist in any recognizable form. Life expectancy was under fifty.

These were the starting conditions. It is difficult, from the vantage point of 2025, to reconstruct how hopeless they looked at the time. Not difficult in the sense of emotionally difficult — difficult in the sense of epistemologically difficult. The gap between what any reasonable observer in 1965 would have predicted for these countries and what actually happened is one of the largest forecasting errors in human history.

What actually happened is this. Between 1965 and 2025, Singapore's GDP per capita grew from five hundred dollars to over eighty thousand dollars — a 160-fold increase. It now exceeds that of the United States. Life expectancy rose from sixty-six to eighty-four. The country that had been expelled from Malaysia as an unviable rump state became, by multiple international rankings, one of the most functional societies on earth: first in infrastructure, first in education, first in government effectiveness, first in ease of doing business.

South Korea's GDP per capita grew from roughly one hundred and fifty dollars to over thirty-three thousand dollars. It became the world's largest producer of semiconductors and the most connected society on the planet, with an internet infrastructure that makes American broadband look like a historical reenactment. It transitioned from military dictatorship to functional democracy without economic regression — a feat that the development literature of the 1970s had argued was essentially impossible.

China lifted more than eight hundred million people out of poverty between 1980 and 2015 — the largest and fastest reduction in material deprivation in the history of the species. Its economy grew from roughly two percent of global GDP to roughly eighteen percent. It built, in thirty years, a high-speed rail network longer than the rest of the world's combined. It constructed cities — dozens of them, each larger than most European capitals — on schedules that Western infrastructure projects would have spent in environmental review alone.

Vietnam, which American policymakers in the 1970s had written off as a permanently damaged society, grew at an average of six to seven percent per year for three decades. Its poverty rate fell from over seventy percent to under six percent. It became a manufacturing destination as Western companies diversified supply chains away from China — not because Vietnam was cheap, but because it was competent.

The speed of these transformations has no precedent. Western Europe's industrialization took roughly two centuries, from the first stirrings of the Industrial Revolution in the late eighteenth century to broadly shared prosperity in the late twentieth. The Asian economies described above compressed this process into forty to sixty years. They did not skip stages. They ran through them at a speed that the Western understanding of development had no category for, because the Western understanding of development had assumed, quietly and without much examination, that its own pace was the natural pace — that history moved at the speed of history, and history's speed was European.

It is not. History's speed is whatever the most serious people in the room decide it is.

One of the quietest and most consequential facts of the past half-century is that most Westerners do not know these numbers. Not in the sense of having forgotten them. In the sense of never having encountered them in the first place. The educational systems that produced the American businessman on the train from Hong Kong — and that produced the readers of this book, and that produced the author of this book — did not teach this history. The media environment in which those same people swim did not cover this history except in fragments: a trade-war headline here, a human-rights story there, a technology-transfer panic somewhere else. The fragments were accurate, often. What they were not was assembled. No institution in the Western information ecosystem had the incentive or the inclination to present the full arc, because the full arc told a story that was uncomfortable for the ecosystem's assumed audience. The story was not that the West had failed. The story was that the West had been dramatically and comprehensively outperformed at the thing it had spent two centuries insisting it was best at — building prosperous, stable, functional societies — and most Westerners had not been informed that the contest was happening.

The American on the train was not unusual. He was typical. His ignorance was not a personal failing. It was the predictable output of a system that had not been designed to tell him the truth because the system itself did not know it, or did not want to know it, or had structured its incentives such that knowing it was professionally costly. None of these explanations is flattering. All of them are accurate.

This is the context in which the subject of the next section becomes legible. If Asia's transformation were a collection of miracles — inexplicable one-offs, dependent on unique historical accidents, unrepeatable — then the appropriate Western response would be admiration at a safe distance. Interesting. Remarkable. Not relevant to us. But the transformations were not miracles. They were the products of specific decisions made by specific people operating within specific frameworks. The frameworks varied — China was not Singapore, Vietnam was not South Korea — but the principles that underlay them did not. Across wildly different cultures, political systems, and starting conditions, the same patterns appeared: disciplined governance with long time horizons, investment in human capital as the primary national resource, a refusal to let ideology override outcomes, and a willingness to do what worked regardless of what the rest of the world thought about it.

The man who understood these principles most clearly, who articulated them most directly, and who applied them most successfully over the longest period, was the prime minister of a tiny island at the southern tip of the Malay Peninsula. In 1965, when Singapore was supposed to fail, he was forty-one years old and had already decided it would not.
