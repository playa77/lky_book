At this point a certain kind of reader will have accumulated a certain kind of objection, and it is better to name it than to let it sit unaddressed. The objection goes something like this: the preceding pages have described four authoritarian or semi-authoritarian governments — Singapore, China, Vietnam, and the Park Chung-hee era in South Korea — in terms that are, if not admiring, at least substantially more positive than the reader has been conditioned to expect. The objection's stronger form: this is an apology for authoritarianism dressed up as pragmatism. Its weaker form: even if the outcomes are real, outcomes achieved by repression are not achievements worth celebrating, and focusing on the outcomes while minimizing the repression is a category error. Both forms are serious. Both deserve an answer.

The answer begins with a distinction that is easy to state and difficult to maintain: describing how something works is not the same as endorsing it. An oncologist who explains the mechanism by which a tumor grows is not advocating for the tumor. An engineer who analyzes why a bridge collapsed is not arguing that bridges should collapse. The purpose of the preceding chapters has not been to argue that Singapore's political system, or China's, or Vietnam's, is morally superior to the alternatives. The purpose has been to establish a fact that is uncomfortable precisely because it is a fact: something worked in these places. The outcomes — the GDP growth, the poverty reduction, the life expectancy gains, the infrastructure, the institutional competence — are not ideological claims. They are measurements. To refuse to look at the measurements because the governments that produced them are unsavory is not a moral position. It is an epistemological one, and it is the kind of position that makes it impossible to learn anything from anyone already dismissed as an enemy. If the only evidence admitted is evidence from systems already approved of, the exercise is not analysis. It is confirmation.

This is not an abstract point. During the Cold War, the United States allied with genuinely monstrous regimes — Suharto's Indonesia, Pinochet's Chile, Mobutu's Zaire — while simultaneously lecturing the world about democracy and human rights. The contradiction was obvious to everyone except Americans, who had been raised to believe that their country's interventions were always, on balance, a net force for good. The point here is not to relitigate the Cold War. The point is that every society has a self-serving narrative about its own virtue, and the first step toward clear thinking is to notice when someone is inside theirs. The Western reader who reflexively dismisses Singapore's achievements because Singapore is not a liberal democracy is doing exactly what the Chinese nationalist does when he dismisses Western achievements because the West is imperialist. Both are protecting a worldview from evidence. Both are wrong for the same reason.

This book — the construction is deliberate — is built on a question that most Western analysis of Asia has been careful not to ask. The question is not "why did Asia succeed?" That question contains its own answer: Asia succeeded because it copied the West, which makes the West the protagonist of the story even when the story is about someone else. The actual question, and the one that organizes everything that follows, is this: if societies that started with less — less capital, less technology, less institutional inheritance, less of nearly everything the West had — managed to build more in a single lifetime than the West has built in two, what does that tell us about the societies that started with more and built less?

The question is not rhetorical. It has answers, and the answers are not flattering, and a book that refused to state them plainly would be wasting its reader's time. But answering it honestly requires a lens — someone who can see both systems clearly, who has no incentive to flatter either, and who spent a career demonstrating that the difference between successful and unsuccessful societies is not ideology or culture or historical luck but something more mundane and more actionable: the quality of the decisions made by the people in charge. That sounds like a truism. It is not. The entire edifice of Western political thought since the Enlightenment has been built on the premise that the most important variable is the system — democracy, rights, rule of law — and that outcomes follow automatically from getting the system right. LKY's life's work was a sustained demonstration that this premise is incomplete at best and delusional at worst. The system matters. The people running it matter more.

Lee Kuan Yew was that person. He was educated in the West and governed in the East. He admired British institutions and refused to be bound by British sentimentality. He built a Western-style legal system and used it to restrict political opposition. He welcomed multinational capital and suppressed labor unions. He created one of the most functional societies on earth using methods that made democratic theorists and authoritarian apologists equally uncomfortable. He was, in other words, a pragmatist — not in the weak sense of someone who compromises between opposing positions, but in the strong sense of someone who evaluates every policy by one criterion and one criterion alone: does it work? And if it does not, what will?

The next chapter is about what he saw when he turned that criterion on the West.
