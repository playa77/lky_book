There is a seductive argument, heard with increasing frequency in policy circles and technology conferences, that goes roughly as follows: the institutions of democratic governance are struggling to manage the complexity of the modern world, but AGI will solve this. It will process the information that human civil servants cannot, model the tradeoffs that political leaders cannot, identify the inefficiencies that legacy systems cannot, and deliver the services that creaking bureaucracies cannot. The technology that is disrupting the economy will also upgrade the state. The challenge is to survive the transition; the destination, on the other side of it, is governance that finally works.

This argument is seductive because it resolves the cognitive dissonance between the magnitude of the AGI challenge and the absence of institutional preparation for it. If the technology will fix the institutions, then the absence of institutional preparation is not a crisis. It is a waiting period. The only thing required is to hold on until the technology arrives, at which point the technology will do what the institutions could not.

LKY would have recognized this argument immediately, because it is the argument that every country that failed to develop made about the resource that was supposed to rescue it. The resource-rich country that neglects its institutions because the oil will pay for everything discovers, when the oil price collapses, that it has neither oil revenue nor institutional capacity — and the oil, while it lasted, had removed the pressure to build the capacity. The country that discovers a strategic commodity and uses the revenue to subsidize consumption rather than to invest in infrastructure discovers, when the commodity is no longer strategic, that it has consumed its advantage without converting it into anything durable. The pattern is consistent across every domain in which an external windfall has been presented as a substitute for institutional competence: the windfall does not substitute for competence. It masks the absence of it, and the masking makes the absence worse, because the period during which the windfall was available was the period during which institutional capacity could have been built, and was not.

AGI is not a windfall. It is a force multiplier. The distinction is the distinction LKY drew for every transformative technology that passed through Singapore during his lifetime — containerized shipping, satellite communications, computerized finance — and it is the distinction on which the argument of this section depends. A force multiplier amplifies the quality of the force it is multiplying. Applied to a competent institution, it produces extraordinary outcomes: the Port of Singapore Authority, already well-managed, used containerization to become the world's busiest transshipment hub. Applied to an incompetent institution, it produces extraordinary failure, at extraordinary speed, at a scale the incompetence alone could never have achieved.

The mechanism is not mysterious. An institution that makes bad decisions slowly will, with AGI, make bad decisions quickly. An institution that cannot distinguish relevant information from noise will, with AGI, be flooded with information it cannot evaluate, and its inability to evaluate it will be hidden behind the machine's confidence in presenting it. An institution whose procedures were designed for a different era will, with AGI, automate those procedures — embedding the obsolescence into code, making it faster, more consistent, and harder to change. An institution whose staff lack the technical competence to understand the systems they are deploying will deploy them anyway, because the political pressure to adopt the technology will exceed the institutional capacity to assess it, and the result will be automated errors that no one inside the institution can detect, explain, or correct.

The competence prerequisite is not a modest caveat to an otherwise optimistic assessment. It is the entire assessment. AGI deployed into a competent institutional environment amplifies competence. AGI deployed into an incompetent institutional environment amplifies incompetence. There is no neutral case, because the deployment itself is a decision made by the institution, and the quality of that decision is determined by the quality of the institution making it. An institution that cannot competently procure an IT system will not competently procure an AGI system. An institution that cannot manage the contractors who build its websites will not manage the contractors who build its AI. The technology is only as good as the governance that surrounds it, and the governance that surrounds it, in the Western democracies at the moment the AGI transition begins, is the governance that Part II of this book described — declining in capacity, captured by short-term incentives, optimized for consumption rather than construction.

The specific forms that amplified dysfunction will take are already visible in the early interactions between government and AI. The Dutch childcare benefits scandal, in which an algorithmic fraud-detection system falsely accused more than twenty thousand families of wrongdoing over nearly a decade, ruining lives and toppling a government, was not a failure of the technology in isolation. It was a failure of the institutions that deployed it — institutions that could not detect the errors the system was making, could not respond to the complaints the victims were filing, and could not override the system's outputs because no one inside the institutions had the authority to contradict the algorithm's conclusions. Automated decision systems deployed in welfare administration, immigration processing, and criminal justice have demonstrated, across multiple jurisdictions, the same pattern: the technology accelerates the processing of cases while replicating and hardening the biases embedded in the training data and the institutional procedures that generated it. The errors that a human caseworker makes one at a time, over weeks, with opportunities for appeal and correction, the automated system makes thousands at a time, in seconds, with no opportunity for appeal because no human in the institution understands the system well enough to evaluate its outputs. The competence gap — between the institution's capacity to deploy the technology and its capacity to govern it — transforms a slow, correctable injustice into a fast, uncorrectable one.

The same amplification dynamic applies to regulatory capture, to surveillance, to the use of AI in military and intelligence contexts, to the concentration of market power, and to every other domain in which governance quality determines whether a technology serves the public or the interests that capture the public's institutions. A regulator that cannot stand up to a regulated industry today will not stand up to that industry when the industry is armed with AGI. A legislature that cannot write effective technology law today will not write effective AI law when the technology is moving faster and the expertise gap wider. An electorate that cannot hold its government accountable for the decisions it already makes will not hold it accountable for the decisions an AGI system recommends — because the recommendation will arrive with the authority of computation, and the authority of computation, in a society that has ceased to trust its institutions, is the only authority left.

The naive view — that AGI arrives as an upgrade to broken systems — gets the relationship exactly backwards. The systems are not broken because they lack the computational capacity to function well. They are broken because they lack the institutional capacity to function well, and computational capacity amplifies institutional capacity in whichever direction it is already pointed. The only way AGI improves governance is if governance is improved before AGI is deployed, or simultaneously with deployment, through institutional reforms that address the capacity deficits, the incentive misalignments, and the accountability gaps that the technology will otherwise accelerate.

This is the competence prerequisite, and it shifts the question from "how do we deploy AGI in government?" to "how do we build governments capable of deploying AGI?" The first question assumes the institutions are adequate and asks how to add a tool. The second question recognizes that the institutions are not adequate, and asks whether they can be made adequate in the time available before the tool arrives and begins multiplying whatever adequacy is present. The answer to that question, in most Western democracies at the moment of the AGI transition, is not reassuring. But the question itself is the minimum requirement for seriousness — because the alternative, deploying AGI into broken institutions and expecting repair, is not a strategy. It is a repetition of the pattern that produced the broken institutions in the first place: the deferral of institutional reform, disguised as technological progress, producing outcomes that are worse than the outcomes the reform was supposed to prevent.
