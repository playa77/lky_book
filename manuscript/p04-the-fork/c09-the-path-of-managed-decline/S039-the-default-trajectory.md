In 1914, the Austro-Hungarian Empire was a great power. It had governed central Europe for centuries, commanded an army of three million men, and presided over one of the continent's cultural capitals in Vienna. Four years later it no longer existed. But the instructive thing about the Habsburg collapse is not the speed of the dissolution. It is the duration of the prelude.

The empire had been in managed decline for decades. Its industrial base was falling behind Germany's and Britain's. Its military was underfunded by a parliament that could not agree on defense spending because its ethnic factions could not agree on anything. Its political institutions were designed to prevent any faction from exercising power at the expense of any other, which meant they were designed to prevent anyone from governing. The empire survived by inertia — the accumulated weight of centuries of rule, the absence of a credible alternative, the fact that everyone who mattered was comfortable enough to prefer the present to the unknown. The decay was gradual, almost imperceptible year by year, and the empire's response to each new symptom was consistent: it deferred the decision. In 1866 it lost a war to Prussia and did not reform. In 1908 it annexed Bosnia and could not govern it. In 1914 it marched into a war it could not win because the alternative was admitting it had been declining for fifty years and had done nothing about it.

This is the default trajectory for any complex institution that has lost the capacity for self-correction but has not yet experienced consequences severe enough to force it. It does not collapse. It erodes. The erosion is gradual enough that each year's degradation is too small to justify the cost of reversing it, and the cumulative degradation, over decades, is large enough to make reversal impossible. The institution survives as long as the environment does not demand more than it can provide. The moment the environment demands more — a war, a crisis, a technology that rewrites the terms of competition — the survival is revealed to have been a statistical artifact. The institution had not been surviving. It had been waiting, in a conducive environment, for a test that would expose the distance between its capacities and its requirements.

The West, at the moment the AGI transition begins, is approaching the end of that waiting period.

The trajectory is not a mystery. The mechanisms that produce it are the mechanisms Part II described — the comfort trap, the corrector's paradox, the three proximate drivers of welfare as sedative, ideology as substitute for results, and rights untethered from obligations — operating over time scales longer than a single political career and therefore invisible to the institutions that would need to see them to respond. Declining state capacity, measured not in budget allocations but in the ability to execute complex projects, absorb surprise, and sustain focus across election cycles, is a gradual process. Each year, slightly fewer capable people enter public service because the private sector pays more and the political environment punishes seriousness. Each year, slightly more institutional knowledge departs with retirees and is not replaced. Each year, the gap between what voters expect the state to deliver and what it can deliver widens slightly, and the response has been to borrow the difference rather than close the gap.

Growing inequality follows the same pattern. The mechanisms — education that sorts by parental income, housing markets that concentrate opportunity, tax systems that favor income from capital over income from labor — are individually rational, politically defended, and cumulative. Each year, slightly more productive assets flow to slightly fewer recipients, and the political power that accompanies concentrated wealth makes correction harder. The result is not revolutionary. It is Habsburg: an elite comfortable enough to prefer the present to any alternative, a population whose economic purpose erodes at a rate slow enough to generate resignation rather than mobilization, and a political system in which the party of the comfortable and the party of the resentful alternate power without altering the trajectory.

Fragmenting cohesion is the third vector, and it is the one that makes the first two irreversible. A society whose members do not trust one another cannot agree on how to distribute burdens, cannot sustain institutions that require shared sacrifice, and cannot mobilize collective action against threats that do not discriminate by political affiliation. The mechanisms — algorithmic media optimized for engagement, immigration policies that import diversity without investing in integration, economic transformation that leaves groups behind without providing a path back — are well-known. What is less discussed is that each operates on the same gradual schedule as the other erosion vectors: effects invisible in any given year, unmistakable over any given decade. The decline of institutional trust, the rise of affective polarization, the replacement of shared identity with antagonistic identities — each year, slightly more connective tissue degrades, and the degradation is not repaired because the institutions that would repair it are the institutions whose legitimacy the degradation has eroded.

The AGI transition does not create any of these vectors. It accelerates them. Every mechanism of erosion that operates gradually under normal conditions operates at compression under AGI conditions because the technology does not respect the time scales that democratic institutions and social processes require. AGI commoditizes cognitive labor — and the West's economic model, built on the assumption that cognitive labor would always be the human advantage, loses its foundation not in a generation but in a cycle. AGI concentrates wealth — and the mechanisms that already concentrate wealth toward the owners of productive assets concentrate it faster when the productive asset is an intelligence that can substitute for labor across most cognitive domains. AGI fragments cohesion — and the fragmentation accelerates when the primary medium of social interaction is an algorithmic platform whose engagement-maximizing content reliably turns out to be the content that deepens division.

The end state is not collapse. It is functional irrelevance. The country that follows the default trajectory will still exist, in the sense that its territory will be governed by a government that collects taxes and issues passports. But it will have lost the capacity to shape its own circumstances — economically, because value creation will occur elsewhere; strategically, because the decisions that matter will be made by actors whose interests are not its own; and culturally, because a society that cannot agree on what it is cannot agree on what it wants to become. It will be a Habsburg outcome: comfortable enough to persist, incapable enough to matter, waiting, in an environment that is becoming less conducive, for a test that it will not pass.

This is the default path. It requires no decisions. It is the path a society follows when it does nothing, which is to say when it does what it has been doing for decades because doing otherwise would require acknowledging that decades of doing things the same way produced the position from which something different must now be done. The alternative exists. It is the subject of the chapter that follows this one. But the argument for the alternative cannot be understood until the default is understood, and the default cannot be understood as a catastrophe to be prevented. It must be understood as a trajectory already in motion, whose destination is visible from its present position, and whose arrival is guaranteed unless something actively intervenes.
