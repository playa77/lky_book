The mechanisms of erosion have been described. The question that remains is how they interact over time — not as abstractions, but as events that will occur in a sequence, at a pace, with consequences that compound because the society experiencing them cannot respond to any one of them before the next one arrives. The default trajectory is not a steady, gentle slope. It is a series of thresholds, each of which, once crossed, accelerates the approach to the next. The timeline is not measured in decades. It is measured in years, because the technology that drives the acceleration is advancing on a schedule that does not respect the deliberative time scales of democratic institutions. The sequence has three movements. They overlap. They reinforce each other. And the society that enters the first movement will not exit the third.

The first movement is the technology's acceleration past the capacity of institutions to regulate, redistribute, or even understand it. The AGI transition is not a single event — a product launch, a capability demonstration, a moment after which everything is different. It is a continuous process in which capabilities advance faster than the institutions that would need to govern them can be designed, staffed, and empowered. The gap between what the technology can do and what the institutions can control widens with every capability advance, and the capability advances are coming on a schedule measured in months, not years. The EU AI Act, years in negotiation and adoption, will be obsolete before its compliance deadlines arrive — not because it was poorly drafted, but because the technology it regulates will have advanced past the assumptions it was drafted under before anyone is required to comply with it. The same dynamic applies to every regulatory framework, every policy response, every institutional adaptation. The institutions move at the speed of legislation and litigation. The technology moves at the speed of engineering and capital. The gap between them is not a problem to be solved. It is a condition to be lived with, and it is widening.

The second movement is the concentration of wealth that accompanies the concentration of technological capability. The mechanisms are the mechanisms described earlier in this book: AGI commoditizes cognitive labor, which is the source of income for the professional classes that have been the primary beneficiaries of the knowledge economy. The returns to capital — specifically, the capital that owns the AGI systems and the infrastructure they run on — rise relative to the returns to labor, not gradually but discontinuously, because the substitution of machine intelligence for human intelligence does not happen gradually. A capability that required a team of analysts, lawyers, or programmers last year requires an API call this year. The firm that employed the team captures the entire productivity increase as a reduction in labor costs. The displaced workers capture nothing, because they are no longer workers. The wealth that the technology creates flows to the owners of the technology, and the owners of the technology are a small number of firms and the even smaller number of people who control them, concentrated in a small number of jurisdictions whose tax systems are not designed to capture the value because the value was not supposed to be capturable by a machine.

The term that economists use for the distribution that results from this process is a vertical Gini coefficient — vertical, because the concentration operates across the entire income distribution rather than within segments of it, pulling the top fraction of a percent away from everyone else at a rate that makes the inequalities of the past century look like rounding errors. The political consequence of vertical concentration is not revolt. It is resignation. The wealth is too concentrated to be redistributed by the political mechanisms that exist, because the political mechanisms that exist are the mechanisms that the concentrated wealth has captured or can outlast. The institutions that were supposed to check concentration — antitrust enforcement, progressive taxation, labor market regulation — are the institutions that the erosion of state capacity has hollowed out, and the period during which they could have been rebuilt was the period during which the concentration was already accelerating.

The third movement is the tearing of the social fabric. This is not a metaphor. It is a description of what happens to a society when a large portion of its population loses economic purpose. The mechanisms that hold a society together — shared experience, mutual obligation, the belief that the system is fair enough to be worth participating in — depend, in ways that the prosperous members of prosperous societies find easy to forget, on the availability of work that is valued by the economy. Work is not only income. It is identity, structure, community, and dignity. When it disappears, the things that depend on it disappear too, not because people choose to stop participating in society but because the society has stopped providing them with a reason to participate that the society itself considers legitimate.

The sequence is not linear, because the three movements interact. The wealth concentrated by the second movement funds the technology acceleration of the first — the capital that the displacement generates is reinvested in the capabilities that generate more displacement. The institutional incapacity exposed by the first movement prevents the redistribution that could slow the second — the regulatory frameworks that could tax the concentrated wealth and redistribute it as income support or retraining are the frameworks that the incapacity prevents from being designed and enforced. The social fabric torn by the third movement removes the political pressure that could force the institutional repair that would address the first two — a population that has lost economic purpose and civic identity does not organize to demand institutional reform. It retreats, or it rages, but it does not govern.

The end state of the sequence, the destination toward which each movement pushes, is the condition described at the opening of this chapter: functional irrelevance. A country that still exists, in the formal sense, but has lost the capacity to shape its own circumstances — economically, because the value creation happens elsewhere; politically, because the decisions that matter are made by actors whose interests are not its own; socially, because a population that cannot agree on what it is cannot agree on what it wants to become. This is not a prediction of apocalypse. It is a description of what happens to any complex system when its adaptive capacity is exceeded by the rate of change in its environment, and the system, instead of adapting, continues to behave as though the environment were the one it was designed for. The tear is not the end. The tear is what happens on the way to the end, and the end is the Habsburg outcome: comfortable enough to persist, incapable enough to matter, waiting for a test that it will not pass.
