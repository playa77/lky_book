When the world's first nuclear reactor went critical beneath the University of Chicago's football stadium in December 1942, no regulatory framework existed for the technology it had just demonstrated. The gap between capability and governance was absolute. Within four years, the United States Congress had passed the Atomic Energy Act, establishing a specialized agency with the statutory authority, technical competence, and institutional independence to govern a technology whose destructive potential exceeded anything in human experience. The speed of the institutional response was proportional to the stakes. AGI presents a governance challenge of comparable urgency, with a critical difference: the institutions that would need to mount the response are in worse condition than those that governed the nuclear transition, and the time available to do so is shrinking faster than the regulatory processes designed to address it.

The regulatory model AGI requires is borrowed from the one domain where the consequences of failure are catastrophic and the margin for error is zero. Nuclear-safety-level regulation means three things concretely. First, pre-deployment authorization: no system above a defined capability threshold enters operation without demonstrating safety to an empowered regulator's satisfaction. The developer bears the burden of proof, not the regulator. Second, continuous monitoring: the technology evolves at speeds that make periodic inspection obsolete. Regulatory oversight must be real-time, embedded in the development process, and backed by the technical capacity to evaluate systems whose internal operations are opaque even to their creators. Third, emergency intervention authority: the regulator must possess the statutory power to halt deployment, revoke authorization, and mandate modification without the procedural delays that make existing technology regulators spectators to the industries they ostensibly oversee. Each of these requirements contradicts the default model of technology regulation in the West, which is reactive, under-resourced, and calibrated for products that do not improve themselves. AGI is not a product in that sense. It is a capability whose improvement is endogenous, and the regulatory architecture that governs it must be designed for a technology that will be more powerful at the end of the regulatory process than it was at the beginning.

The regulatory architecture cannot function without a solution to the economic distribution problem that AGI creates. The mobility-of-intelligence problem described in Part III — AGI runs in data centers, serves via the internet, is not anchored to any jurisdiction — means the default distribution of AGI's economic returns concentrates them among the technology's owners and the capital that finances it. LKY's principle, applied to intelligence as it was to land, capital, and labor, is straightforward: whoever controls the critical resource controls the society that depends on it. The intervention that prevents intelligence-capital concentration from producing a society of owners and spectators is pre-distribution: institutionalizing the broad distribution of economic returns before concentration occurs, rather than attempting redistribution after the fact through tax systems that concentrated wealth has already learned to navigate. The mechanism is an AGI dividend, structured as a sovereign wealth fund seeded by levies on AGI-derived revenues and returns to AI-compute capital. The model exists: Singapore's GIC and Temasek were seeded from the country's high-growth decades, converting temporary economic surplus into permanent national assets whose returns benefit every citizen. An AGI dividend applies the same logic to the most economically transformative technology in history — not redistribution, which takes from those who have already captured the gains, but pre-distribution, which ensures the gains are broadly shared from the moment they are generated.

Compute is the strategic resource of the AGI era. Access to large-scale computation determines who can train frontier models, who can deploy them, and at what cost external parties can audit or constrain them. Treating compute as a strategic resource means three things. First, sovereign investment in compute infrastructure: government must ensure the capacity to develop and evaluate frontier systems is not concentrated in a small number of private actors whose incentives do not fully align with public interest. Second, compute-access requirements as a condition of market participation: developers of frontier systems must provide regulated access to compute for auditing, safety evaluation, and independent research, on terms that do not make oversight dependent on the cooperation of the overseen. Third, strategic export controls on the most advanced compute hardware and the fabrication technology that produces it, preventing the diffusion of capability to actors whose governance frameworks cannot manage it. The principle, again, is LKY's: identify the resource on which everything else depends, and organize the institutional architecture around its control. For the nuclear age it was fissile material. For the information age it was data. For the AGI age it is compute.

The institutional form these mechanisms require is not a radical departure from Western tradition. It is an acknowledgment that the conditions Western institutions were designed to govern have changed. The proposal is for small, technically literate agencies — specialized, well-compensated, statutorily empowered — modeled on central banks, aviation safety authorities, and nuclear regulators that Western democracies already use to govern domains where the cost of failure is catastrophic and the speed of the domain exceeds the speed of ordinary legislative process. As established in the talent proposals, these agencies must recruit from the same talent pool as the industry they regulate, at competitive compensation, with statutory authority to make binding decisions at the technology's own pace. Their legitimacy derives from competence, their mandates from the legislature that creates them, and their accountability from operational transparency and specifiable success criteria. Democratic accountability and governance speed are in genuine tension at this margin, and the tension cannot be resolved by pretending that existing democratic procedures can operate at AGI speed. The resolution is to accept the delegation of authority that centralized technical regulation requires, while building accountability mechanisms — sunset provisions, legislative oversight committees with technical staff, judicial review of regulatory decisions — that prevent delegation from becoming abdication.

The costs of this architecture are real and must be stated, not minimized. Regulated entities will resist pre-deployment authorization, arguing it slows innovation — an argument that assumes innovation at maximum speed is an unqualified good, which it is not when the innovation in question is capable of displacing the society that authorizes it. The AGI dividend requires levies that will be resisted by the entities on which they are imposed, and the resistance will take the form of arguments about competitiveness and jurisdiction-shopping that have substance and must be addressed through international coordination. Strategic compute policy imposes costs on research and commercial activity that would prefer to operate without constraint, and the line between legitimate regulation and stifling overreach requires calibration. Each of these costs must be weighed against the cost of the default trajectory, which is the irreversible loss of governing capacity in the face of a technology that will not pause while the institutions catch up. The comparison is not between this architecture and a costless alternative. It is between this architecture and the managed decline the previous chapter described, whose costs are larger, distributed more broadly, and permanent.
