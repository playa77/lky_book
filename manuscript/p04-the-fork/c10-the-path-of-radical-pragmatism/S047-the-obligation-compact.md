On any morning in Singapore, several hundred nineteen-year-old men report to Pulau Tekong for Basic Military Training. They include the sons of cabinet ministers and the sons of hawkers. There is no exemption for wealth, no deferment for elite education, no buyout for the connected. For two years they share bunks, rations, and physical exhaustion. For twenty years after that they return annually for reserve training, until age forty. Every one of them, regardless of what they would rather be doing.

This is, in Lee Kuan Yew's assessment, the single most important institution Singapore built -- more important than the port, more important than the housing program, more important than the sovereign wealth funds. The reason is not primarily military, though the military necessity was real -- a city-state of two million, abruptly independent, surrounded by larger neighbors with recent memories of confrontation. The reason is social. National service takes the children of the elite and the children of the poor and subjects them to the same demands, the same physical standards, the same authority, under conditions where performance determines standing. It is the state's answer to a question no other institution asks: what does a citizen owe the society that protects them?

No Western democracy imposes anything remotely comparable. The United States ended conscription in 1973, Britain in 1960, France in 2001, Germany in 2011. In every case the decision was popular. Conscription compels people to do something they would rather not, at an age when they have other plans. But the question the West stopped asking when it abolished the draft is the one that matters: if citizens owe nothing enforceable to the state that guarantees their security, their property, and their liberty, what exactly binds them to it? What obligation, beyond the occasional jury summons and a tax return that a substantial fraction of the population does not pay, connects a citizen to the political community whose benefits they consume?

The answer, increasingly, is nothing. The Western social contract has become a one-way transaction. The state provides education, infrastructure, security, legal order, social insurance, and the expectation of economic opportunity. The citizen receives. Rights have expanded continuously. Obligations have contracted to the point of vanishing. The result is not freedom. It is what Singapore's founding generation would have called a subsidy -- an arrangement in which one party contributes and the other consumes, sustained only as long as the contributing party can afford it.

The obligation compact that must be rebuilt has two components. Neither is sufficient alone.

The first is universal national service -- not universal military service, though a military track remains an option within it. It means a period of mandatory public contribution, twelve to eighteen months, between secondary education and university or career entry, in which every citizen serves in a capacity that benefits the broader society. Military service is one track. Civil infrastructure is another: environmental restoration, elderly care, disaster response, public health deployment. Digital service is a third: cybersecurity, digital literacy training, technology deployment for public agencies. The form is secondary. The requirement that everyone serves, that no one buys out, that the children of the powerful share the conditions of the children of the powerless -- that is the point.

The mechanism works because it is universal. Exemptions destroy it. A system that allows the wealthy to avoid service through private-education deferments or medical exemptions purchased from compliant doctors entrenches exactly the class division it is designed to dissolve. Universality is the active ingredient. Remove it, and national service becomes a tax on the poor paid in time while the rich pay in money. That arrangement is not obligation. It is the existing arrangement with a uniform.

The second component is fiscal. In every Western democracy, a substantial fraction of the adult population -- typically the bottom forty to fifty percent of earners -- pays no net federal income tax. The rich pay, and they complain about paying. The poor do not pay, and they are told this is fairness. Both claims are false. The rich are told that taxation is theft when it is the price of the stability that makes their wealth possible. The poor are told that exemption from contribution is liberation when it is the removal of their stake in the system. A citizen who pays nothing has no basis for demanding accountability. They are not a participant. They are a recipient, and recipients do not govern.

The fiscal obligation requires that every citizen with any income contributes something -- even a token amount -- to the public treasury. The rate can be progressive. The burden can be calibrated. But the zero line must be eliminated. A citizen who pays a hundred dollars a year in federal tax has genuine standing to question a hundred-billion-dollar appropriation. A citizen who pays nothing has no standing to question anything. They have been disenfranchised by the very policy sold to them as fairness.

The reciprocity principle governs both components. The state earns the right to demand contribution by demonstrating competence. A government that cannot deliver basic services, secure its borders, or maintain fiscal discipline has no moral standing to demand service or payment from its citizens. The compact is bilateral -- obligation flows in both directions, and failure on either side breaks the mechanism. This is why the education reform of the previous section and the obligation compact of this section cannot be separated. The state must demonstrate that it is worth contributing to -- worth serving, worth funding, worth the sacrifice that citizenship in a serious country demands. If it cannot, it has no claim. If it can, the citizens who refuse to contribute are not exercising freedom. They are free-riding, and free-riding on a functional state is not a right. It is exactly the pathology Lee Kuan Yew spent his career refusing to tolerate.

The costs are real. Universal service delays career entry by twelve to eighteen months. It imposes physical risk -- lower in civilian tracks, but irreducible where military service is involved. It requires an administrative apparatus that Western governments, in their current state, would struggle to operate effectively. The fiscal obligation, however modest, is a tax increase on those who currently pay nothing, and the political resistance to taxing the poor -- however token the amount -- would be ferocious, not from the poor themselves but from the political professionals who have built careers defending them from contribution.

But the costs of inaction are higher. A society whose citizens have no enforceable obligations to one another is not a society. It is a market with a flag, and markets dissolve under pressure. The stress test that AGI represents -- economic dislocation at speed, wealth concentration at scale, institutional overload at every level -- is the kind of pressure that dissolves markets and leaves only what was built before the pressure arrived. The obligation compact is what gets built before the pressure arrives. Afterward is too late.

The education reforms of the previous section produce capable citizens. The obligation reforms proposed here bind those capable citizens to one another by something stronger than convenience. Together they constitute the citizen contract that a serious country requires: citizens will be equipped, and they will be asked. The next section addresses the third element without which neither education nor obligation can hold -- the deliberate cultivation of a culture that makes both worth defending.
