The previous chapter described a trajectory. This chapter describes an alternative. The distinction is not between optimism and pessimism, or between hope and despair. It is between two structural responses to the same set of facts. The default trajectory is the response of doing nothing — not because nothing is the preferred outcome, but because the institutional incentives to do nothing are stronger than the institutional incentives to act, and the action that would be required is action of a kind that the existing institutional architecture was not designed to perform. The alternative trajectory is the response of doing what LKY would have done: identifying the threat, organizing every available resource around surviving it, and refusing to allow the available options to be limited by the ideological categories that produced the vulnerability.

The proposals that follow are not a political program. They are an engineering response to a structural crisis. The distinction matters because political programs are evaluated by their consistency with a set of prior commitments — to a philosophy of government, to a theory of justice, to a constituency whose interests must be served. Engineering responses are evaluated by a single criterion: whether they work, under the conditions that actually exist, against the threat they are designed to address. The threat, in this case, is the interaction between accelerating AGI capability and declining Western governance capacity described in Part III. The criterion by which the proposals that follow should be evaluated is not whether they are left-wing or right-wing, not whether they are comfortable or uncomfortable, not whether they are consistent with the ideological priors of any particular political tradition. The single relevant question is whether they would increase or decrease the probability that a Western democracy, confronting the AGI transition with its current institutional capacities, retains the ability to govern itself in its own interests at the end of it.

This is an operational declaration, not a rhetorical one. Declaring an emergency rhetorically is easy — every politician in every developed democracy has declared emergencies, real and manufactured, for purposes noble and cynical, and the result has been a devaluation of the word to the point where it no longer signals anything except a desire for attention. Declaring an emergency operationally means something different. It means accepting that the normal procedures by which decisions are made — the deliberation, the consultation, the consensus-building, the veto points, the multi-year legislative processes — are calibrated for conditions in which the cost of delay is measured in marginally suboptimal outcomes, and that the conditions the AGI transition represents are conditions in which the cost of delay is measured in the irreversible loss of governing capacity. It means accepting that the institutional architecture that served well in an era of incremental change is the wrong architecture for an era of discontinuous change, and that the preservation of the society the architecture was designed to serve may require modifying the architecture itself.

This is not an argument for abandoning democratic governance. It is an argument for taking it seriously enough to recognize when its normal operations have become incompatible with its survival. Every democratic society has mechanisms for overriding normal operations when survival is at stake — emergency powers, accelerated legislative procedures, executive authorities that are dormant in peacetime and activated in crisis. The question is not whether such mechanisms exist. The question is whether the political class is capable of recognizing that the conditions for their activation have arrived, and of activating them for the purpose of building institutional capacity rather than for the purpose of consolidating power. The difference between the two purposes is the difference between a society that uses an emergency to strengthen its institutions and a society that uses an emergency to hollow them out. The proposals that follow assume the first purpose. They can be rejected on the grounds that they would not work, or on the grounds that better alternatives exist. They cannot be rejected on the grounds that they require changes to institutional arrangements that were designed for a different era. The era has changed. The arrangements must change with it, or the society they were designed to serve will discover, too late, that the arrangements survived while the society did not.

The method is LKY's method, translated from the context of a small city-state in the developing world to the context of large, wealthy, mature democracies — not because the translation is straightforward, but because the alternative to attempting it is accepting the default trajectory as inevitable, and the default trajectory is not inevitable. It is the default. The distinction, in engineering as in governance, is the distinction between gravity and flight. Gravity is automatic. Flight requires design, energy, and the willingness to accept that the thing that keeps you on the ground is not your friend.

What follows is organized around the domains in which the gap between required capacity and existing capacity is widest, and in which closing the gap produces the largest improvement in the probability of navigating the transition successfully. The proposals are specific, because specificity is the only form of seriousness in policy. They are drawn from mechanisms that have worked, somewhere, at some scale, because pragmatism is the method and evidence is the criterion. And they are offered in the knowledge that every one of them has costs, tradeoffs, and failure modes — because no serious proposal pretends otherwise, and because the relevant comparison is not between each proposal and a perfect alternative. It is between each proposal and the default trajectory, whose costs, tradeoffs, and failure modes the previous chapter described.
