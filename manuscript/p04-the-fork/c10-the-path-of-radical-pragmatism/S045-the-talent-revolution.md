The single highest-leverage intervention in the governance of any society is the quality of the people who govern it. LKY understood this as the first principle from which everything else followed. "You pay peanuts, you get monkeys" was not a quip. It was a design principle, and the design it produced — competitive compensation, rigorous selection, continuous performance evaluation, and the cultural elevation of public service to the status of a national calling — produced the civil service that built Singapore. The Western democracies, which once produced public servants of comparable quality, have spent decades degrading the mechanisms that produced them. The talent revolution that the AGI transition demands is not the creation of new mechanisms from nothing. It is the restoration of mechanisms that the West once possessed, adapted to the conditions of the present, and augmented with the specific capacities that AGI governance requires.

The first mechanism is compensation. The Western governing class is drawn disproportionately from three pools: the independently wealthy, for whom public-sector salaries are irrelevant; the professionally mediocre, for whom public-sector salaries are adequate because private-sector alternatives are limited; and the ideologically committed, for whom public-sector salaries are a sacrifice willingly made. The pool that is systematically excluded is the pool that LKY's Singapore recruited from: the capable and ambitious who are not wealthy, who have private-sector alternatives, and who will not sacrifice their families' financial security for the privilege of public abuse. The solution is not complicated. It is to pay public servants — ministers, senior civil servants, regulators, technical specialists — at rates competitive with the private-sector roles they would otherwise fill. The cost is real. The cost of continuing to staff the institutions that govern the AGI transition with the people who could not get better jobs is larger, and the comparison is not between the cost of competitive compensation and zero. It is between the cost of competitive compensation and the cost of institutional failure, which is measured in the survival of the society the institutions exist to serve.

The second mechanism is selection. The Western democracies select their political leadership through elections and their bureaucratic leadership through civil-service examinations and political appointments. Neither mechanism is designed to identify the specific capacities that AGI governance demands: technical literacy sufficient to evaluate the claims of technical experts, judgment under uncertainty sufficient to make decisions whose consequences will not be known for years, and the ability to maintain institutional focus across electoral cycles when the electoral incentives pull in the opposite direction. The solution is to supplement the existing selection mechanisms with independent assessment bodies — modeled on the audit institutions, central banks, and judicial appointment commissions that Western democracies already use to insulate certain decisions from electoral pressure — that evaluate candidates for senior governance roles against criteria designed for the conditions they will face. These bodies do not replace elections or civil-service procedures. They add a layer of competence filtering that the existing mechanisms were not designed to provide, because the conditions the existing mechanisms were designed for did not require it.

The third mechanism is pipelines. Capable people do not materialize in senior governance roles by accident. They are identified early, developed deliberately, and promoted on the basis of demonstrated performance rather than seniority or political patronage. Singapore's Public Service Commission scholarship program — which identifies high-potential students, funds their education at the world's best universities, and bonds them to public service — is the most systematic example of this approach, but the principle is older than Singapore. Every institution that has ever sustained excellence over multiple generations — the British civil service at its peak, the French grands corps, the American foreign service — built pipelines that identified talent early and developed it over careers. The Western democracies dismantled these pipelines, or allowed them to atrophy, because the political incentives to invest in long-term institutional capacity were weaker than the political incentives to redirect resources to short-term political priorities. Rebuilding them requires accepting that the investment horizon for institutional quality is measured in decades, and that the societies that fail to make the investment will discover, in decades, that they are governed by the people who were available rather than the people who were developed.

The fourth mechanism is institutional design for AGI governance specifically. The capabilities that AGI governance demands — the ability to regulate a technology that evolves faster than legislative processes, the technical competence to evaluate systems whose internal operations are opaque even to their creators, the capacity to enforce compliance against actors whose resources exceed the regulator's — cannot be supplied by generalist civil servants operating through general-purpose institutions. The solution, following the principle that LKY applied to every domain, is to build specialized institutions for specialized functions: small, technically literate, well-compensated agencies with the statutory authority to regulate at the speed the technology requires, staffed by people who could be working in the industry they are regulating and who choose to regulate it because the institution has made regulation a career worth having. The model exists. Central banks regulate financial systems of enormous complexity with staffs that are small relative to the industries they oversee, because the staffs are expert, the mandates are clear, and the institutional cultures value competence over compliance. The same model, applied to AGI, is not a radical departure from Western institutional tradition. It is an extension of it.

The cultural prerequisite is the hardest to specify and the most important to achieve. None of the mechanisms described above will function in a society that treats public service as a consolation prize for those who could not succeed in the private sector, or as a platform for ideological performance, or as a target for contempt that makes capable people unwilling to subject themselves and their families to the experience. Revaluing public service — making it prestigious, respected, and aspirational — is not a policy intervention. It is a cultural change, and cultural changes are slow, diffuse, and impossible to mandate. But they are not impossible to influence. The mechanisms described above — competitive compensation, rigorous selection, visible competence — are themselves instruments of cultural change. A society that demonstrably selects its governors for competence and demonstrably rewards them for performance will, over time, develop a culture that respects the institutions that govern it, because the institutions will have earned the respect. The direction of causation runs both ways, and the place to start is with the mechanisms that are within the society's control.

The proposals are specific, but the principle is singular: governance quality begins with governor quality, and governor quality is a function of who is recruited, how they are selected, how they are developed, and what they are asked to do. The Western democracies once understood this. They built the institutions that proved it. They have forgotten it, and the AGI transition will not wait for them to remember.
