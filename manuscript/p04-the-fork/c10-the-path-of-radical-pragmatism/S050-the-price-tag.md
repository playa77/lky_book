When Singapore became independent in 1965, Lee Kuan Yew allocated forty percent of the national budget to defense. The country's per-capita GDP was below Jamaica's. Its population was largely unskilled. It had no natural resources and no strategic depth — the entire country was smaller than the metropolitan area of most major cities. Spending forty percent of a poor country's budget on soldiers and equipment looked, to outside observers, like paranoia or militarism. It was neither. The Israeli advisors who designed the Singapore Armed Forces from scratch had told LKY what a credible deterrent against two larger neighbors would cost, and LKY had concluded that the cost of not spending it — dependence, vulnerability, eventual absorption — was higher. The arithmetic was brutal and the conclusion was simple. Every proposal in the preceding sections has costs. Stating them honestly is the minimum condition for being taken seriously.

The fiscal costs are the easiest to name and the hardest to pay. Competitive compensation for public servants means government payrolls increase — ministers, senior civil servants, regulators, and technical specialists paid at rates comparable to the private-sector roles they would otherwise fill. The education overhaul requires replacing a teaching workforce whose bottom two-thirds is unqualified for the redefined role, building new assessment infrastructure for competency-based credentialing, and funding the transition during the decade-plus pipeline delay before the first transformed cohort enters the economy. Universal national service means paying every eighteen-year-old a living stipend for twelve to eighteen months, plus the administrative infrastructure to run military, civil, and digital tracks at national scale. Public housing construction at the scale required to make family formation economically viable is the single largest line item in any developed-country budget, and the countries that most need it are the countries that have spent decades underinvesting. The AGI dividend requires levies on the most powerful economic actors in human history, who will resist them with resources that exceed the budgets of the countries attempting to impose them. Sovereign compute infrastructure means government investment in hardware at a scale that currently exists only in the private sector. None of these costs is small. All of them together are large enough that stating a total invites the response that the proposal is unserious — that no democratic government would allocate resources on this scale for these purposes. The response is that democratic governments already allocate resources on this scale. The United States federal government spent more than six trillion dollars in 2024. The question is not whether the resources exist. It is whether the political will exists to direct them toward the survival of the institutions that authorize their expenditure.

The freedom costs are harder to quantify but equally real. Universal national service means the state claims eighteen months of every citizen's life — male and female, no exemptions for wealth, education, or preference. The ethnic quota on housing means the state tells a citizen who they may sell property to. The regulatory architecture proposed for AGI means private companies must demonstrate safety before deployment, submit to continuous monitoring, and accept the authority of a regulator to halt their operations. The paternalism of cultural policy means the state takes positions on family formation, integration, and value transmission that liberal societies have spent generations insisting are private matters. Each of these constraints on individual freedom is a genuine loss. The question is not whether they are losses. It is what the losses purchase, and what the alternative costs. The freedoms at stake in these proposals are not the freedoms that liberal societies were built to protect — speech, conscience, assembly, due process. They are the freedoms that wealthy, secure societies have accumulated over generations of peace and prosperity: the freedom to opt out of obligations while consuming benefits, the freedom to defer costs that will be paid by someone else, the freedom to treat public institutions as service providers whose bills can be forwarded to the next generation. These are real freedoms, and losing them is uncomfortable. But they are not fundamental liberties. They are comfort-zone entitlements, and the cost of preserving them is the cost of the default trajectory.

The LKY calculus applies to every item on this ledger, and it is the only method that produces an honest answer. The cost of not building a competitive civil service is being governed by the people who could not get better jobs. The cost of not reforming education is an entire generation trained for an economy that no longer exists. The cost of not imposing universal obligation is a citizenry that has forgotten what obligation means. The cost of not building housing is a fertility rate that guarantees demographic decline. The cost of not regulating AGI is being governed by the companies that build it, on terms they set, at a speed they control. Each of these costs is larger than the cost of the intervention that prevents it. Not a little larger. Larger by orders of magnitude, and permanent where the intervention is temporary, and structural where the intervention is procedural. This is not an argument for ignoring the costs of action. It is an argument for comparing them to the costs of inaction, which is the comparison the Western fiscal and political discourse has systematically refused to make.

The next section addresses the comparison that underlies all of this accounting, and that the preceding sections have deliberately postponed. The core of LKY's framework is a tradeoff that Western political philosophy treats as a contradiction: individual freedom and collective outcomes are in genuine tension at the margins that matter. The tension is not a problem to be solved. It is a fact to be managed, and the quality of the management determines everything else.
