When Singapore became independent in 1965, roughly half its water came from Malaysia under a treaty signed during the colonial era. The treaty had expiration dates — one in 2011, another in 2061 — and the Malaysian government had made no secret of its willingness to use water as leverage. In the logic of sovereign statecraft, this was a straightforward problem: a vital resource depended on a foreign power that had already demonstrated hostility. The correct response, by any competent strategic analysis, was to eliminate the dependency.

The British had studied desalination during the colonial period and concluded it was prohibitively expensive. The World Bank, approached by Singaporean officials in the early years of independence, offered the same assessment. The technology was immature, the energy costs were punishing, and the economics did not close. The rational course of action, by the standards of conventional cost-benefit analysis, was to wait. Technology improves. Costs decline. The treaties still had decades to run. The prudent administrator defers the large expenditure until the necessity is imminent and the technology is mature.

LKY overruled every one of those arguments. He began catchment expansion in the early 1970s, decades before the first treaty expired, when the technology was primitive and the economics were unfavorable. He committed to membrane-based wastewater recycling — the technology that became NEWater — in the 1990s, when the membranes were expensive and unreliable and the concept of drinking recycled sewage was politically toxic. He built desalination plants when they were still uneconomical relative to imported water, treating the premium not as waste but as an insurance payment. By the time the first water treaty expired in 2011, Singapore had sufficient independent capacity that the non-renewal was a diplomatic footnote rather than an existential crisis. By the time the second treaty expires in 2061, Singapore will very likely be fully water-independent — a city-state on an island with no natural freshwater sources, supplying itself.

The pattern recurs across every domain LKY considered existential. The military was built before the threat materialized, with Israeli advisors flown in while the British were still present and the neighbors were still posturing rather than threatening. The sovereign wealth funds were seeded during the boom decades, when the political pressure to spend the surpluses on current consumption was intense and the argument for saving for future generations was abstract. The education system was overhauled before the economy demanded it, producing engineers and technicians for industries that did not yet exist. The Corrupt Practices Investigation Bureau was given extraordinary powers — including the authority to investigate the prime minister — before corruption had metastasized, when the cost of aggressive enforcement was high and the visible benefit was zero. In every case, the decision to act preceded the necessity to act by years or decades. The cost of acting early was always real and always visible — budget allocations, institutional friction, the political capital required to impose costs on present constituencies for benefits they would not live to see. The cost of acting late — the crisis that never happened, the dependency that was never exploited, the collapse that was averted — was always invisible and always larger.

This is the asymmetry at the core of LKY's method, and it is the asymmetry that Western governance is structurally incapable of recognizing. Democratic politics is optimized for the reverse calculation. The cost of action is immediate and concentrated — a budget line item, a tax increase, a constituency that feels the burden. The cost of inaction is deferred and diffuse — a crisis that may not arrive during the current electoral cycle, a vulnerability that may never be tested, a failure that will be blamed on whoever holds office when it occurs, not on whoever failed to prevent it. The politician who builds the water treatment plant before the crisis pays the political price now and receives no credit later, because later there is no crisis to take credit for averting. The politician who waits gets the inverse: no price now, and if the crisis arrives on someone else's watch, no blame. The incentive structure selects for deferral. It always has. The reason it always has is that the asymmetry is not a bug in democratic governance. It is a feature of any system in which decision-makers are accountable to voters who experience costs in the present and benefits in the future. The discount rate is built into the architecture.

Apply this logic to artificial general intelligence and the calculation is not theoretical. It is mechanical. The cost of acting now — building regulatory capacity, establishing technical competence inside government, creating institutions capable of governing a technology that does not yet fully exist — is real. It requires budgets, talent, political capital, and the willingness to spend all three on a problem that the median voter does not understand and may never experience. The cost of deferring is not real in the present tense. The technology is still being built. The worst-case scenarios are still hypothetical. The lobbyists for inaction point to uncertainty about timelines and capabilities as though uncertainty were an argument for doing nothing, when in fact uncertainty about a potentially catastrophic variable is the strongest argument for preparation that exists. LKY did not know when — or whether — Malaysia would use water as a weapon. He prepared anyway. That is the entire method.

LKY understood this with a clarity that bordered on contempt for those who did not. He did not wait for certainty about Malaysia's intentions before building water independence. He did not wait for clarity about the British withdrawal before building a military. He acted before the necessity became visible, because by the time the necessity is visible to everyone, the window for action has closed. The politician who waits for the voters to demand preparation for a future crisis will wait forever, because voters do not demand preparation for crises they cannot see. The only people who can act before the crisis are the ones who do not need a crisis to act. The question for the societies facing AGI is not whether they possess this capability. The evidence of the preceding chapters suggests they do not. The question is whether they can acquire it in time, and the time is not generous. The asymmetry of error is not a design flaw in the planning method. It is the reason the method exists. Everything else — the institutions, the budgets, the five-decade horizons — follows from it.
