In 2014, the year before LKY died, total global private investment in artificial intelligence was roughly four billion dollars. In 2024, Microsoft alone committed eighty billion dollars to AI infrastructure in a single fiscal year. The following year, the combined capital expenditure of the five largest American technology companies — Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, and Apple — exceeded two hundred billion dollars, nearly all of it directed toward AI compute, data centers, and model training. The number doubled from 2023 to 2024 and doubled again from 2024 to 2025. By early 2026, the capital flowing into artificial intelligence infrastructure exceeds the annual GDP of most of the world's countries, and the people writing the checks are not venture capitalists speculating on a thesis. They are the managements of the largest corporations in the history of capitalism, betting their balance sheets on a specific conviction: that the thing on the other side of this expenditure is not a better search engine or a more persuasive advertisement but a general-purpose cognitive technology that will reorganize the structure of economic production.

This is the number that should concentrate the attention of anyone who thinks the AI debate is about chatbots and image generators. The capital allocation implies a forecast. The forecast, stated plainly by the people making it, is that artificial general intelligence — systems that match or exceed human performance across most economically valuable cognitive tasks — will arrive within the decade. Sam Altman of OpenAI has said "within a few thousand days." Dario Amodei of Anthropic has placed it "in the next few years." Demis Hassabis of DeepMind has pointed to the end of this decade. The Chinese labs — DeepSeek, Alibaba's Qwen team, ByteDance — publish fewer public timelines but have matched or exceeded Western capabilities on successive generations of models with less compute, suggesting either superior efficiency or an understated timetable. The variance between forecasts is real and wide. The direction of every forecast is the same.

The confusion about AI timelines is largely a confusion about what is being measured. If the question is when a machine will surpass human performance at every conceivable cognitive task — the science-fictional AGI of popular imagination — the answer is anyone's guess. If the question is when a machine will be capable of performing most of the cognitive labor that currently requires a human being with a university degree, the answer is already internally visible.

Leading models in early 2026 can write legal briefs that pass the bar exam, placing them in roughly the 90th percentile of human test-takers on a task that requires four years of university and three years of law school. They can generate software from natural-language specifications that passes technical interviews at major technology companies — not toy programs but multi-file applications with database integrations, authentication systems, and deployment configurations. They can produce scientific hypotheses judged plausible by domain experts in molecular biology and materials science, not by reproducing known findings but by proposing novel experiments that researchers then validate in the laboratory. They can reason through multi-step problems in mathematics, physics, and computer science at a level competitive with graduate students at elite institutions. They can translate between dozens of languages with near-human fluency, summarize complex documents with accuracy that exceeds untrained human readers, and generate persuasive prose calibrated to specific audiences. They can do none of these things reliably. They fail unpredictably on tasks a child performs without effort — basic arithmetic, spatial reasoning, the recognition that a statement contradicts a previously stated fact. They hallucinate with confidence: they invent sources, facts, and quotations and present them with the same syntactic authority as verified information. They lack the common sense that human beings acquire through embodied experience, the causal reasoning that comes from manipulating physical objects, the social intelligence that makes a person notice when a conversation has gone wrong. The gap between what they can do and what they cannot is not closing smoothly. It is being closed in discrete jumps, each jump exposing a new class of tasks that were assumed to require human-level intelligence and turn out to be amenable to pattern matching at scale.

But the direction of travel is not in dispute among the people building them. The question is when, not whether, and "when" is collapsing from decades to years. Every major lab has now published a scaling law — a mathematical relationship between compute invested and capability gained — and every published scaling law points upward. No lab has published a scaling law that predicts a plateau. The argument among serious people is about the slope, not the sign.

Two economic facts about this trajectory are worth absorbing before the geopolitical dimension enters the frame. The first is that the economics of training frontier models are so extreme — single training runs costing hundreds of millions of dollars, clusters of specialized chips that require their own power plants, data requirements measured in trillions of tokens — that the number of entities capable of building frontier AI systems is collapsing, not expanding. In 2023, perhaps a dozen organizations worldwide could plausibly train a state-of-the-art model. In 2026, the number is closer to five, and by 2028 it may be three. The most transformative technology in human history is being built by an oligopoly whose members are overwhelmingly American, with one Chinese participant and no European ones. The second fact is that the deployment economics point in the opposite direction. Once trained, frontier models are becoming cheaper to run by an order of magnitude every twelve to eighteen months, and open-weight models — versions released publicly with full access to their parameters — are compressing the gap between frontier and commodity capability to months, not years. The concentration of control at the frontier coexists with an accelerating diffusion of increasingly capable systems into the hands of anyone with an internet connection. Both dynamics are real and operating simultaneously. The strategic problem this creates is that the governance challenge is not one problem but two: how to regulate a concentrated capability that a handful of actors control, and how to regulate a diffuse capability that anyone can access. No existing international governance framework was designed for a technology with both properties simultaneously.

This brings the geopolitical dimension to the foreground, where it belongs. The United States and China are engaged in an AI competition that both governments treat as a national security priority of the first order, and the instruments of that competition — export controls on advanced chips, restrictions on technology transfer, industrial policy directing hundreds of billions of dollars toward domestic semiconductor fabrication — are the instruments of great-power rivalry, not commercial competition. The American strategy, articulated through successive administrations and reinforced by Congress with bipartisan support, is to maintain a decisive lead in frontier AI capability by controlling the global supply of advanced semiconductors and the design tools that produce them. The Chinese strategy is to achieve parity through a combination of state-directed investment, access to alternative supply chains, and efficiency innovations that reduce the compute required to match Western capabilities. Both strategies are working — America maintains a lead, China closes the gap — and neither strategy contains a plausible off-ramp. The dynamic is an arms race in form and substance, and arms races have a known property: they do not produce stable equilibria. They produce acceleration.

Europe occupies a position that would have fascinated LKY. The continent is, by most measures, the third pole of global AI capability. DeepMind was founded in London, Mistral in Paris, Aleph Alpha in Heidelberg. European universities produce world-class AI researchers at a rate comparable to American and Chinese institutions. The European Union has passed the most comprehensive AI regulation in the world, the AI Act, and the enforcement infrastructure is being built. But Europe trains no frontier models. Its largest technology companies are an order of magnitude smaller than their American and Chinese counterparts. The leading European AI startups are partnering with American cloud providers for compute because no European alternative exists at scale. The result is a continent that is a net exporter of AI talent and a net importer of AI capability — a producer of rules and a consumer of systems, with meaningful regulatory power and negligible deployment power. The position is not powerless, but it is dependent, and dependency in a strategic technology has consequences that outlast any regulatory framework.

The honest assessment, stated without drama, is this. The trajectory of AI capabilities is upward and accelerating. The timelines to general-purpose cognitive systems are measured in years, not decades, on the consensus view of the people building them — and the people building them have been consistently right about the direction and consistently wrong, in the optimistic direction, about the pace. The economics are concentrating production into fewer hands while diffusing capability into more. The geopolitical competition between the only two states capable of producing frontier systems is intensifying along lines that make cooperation structurally unlikely and accidents structurally probable. Even the conservative case — AGI arrives in 2035, not 2030; it is less capable than the maximalists predict and arrives more slowly than their median estimate — demands institutional preparation that no developed country has begun. The case for dismissal rests on the belief that every signal is wrong, every lab is mistaken about its own technology, and every capital allocation represents a collective delusion of the largest corporations in history. That belief may turn out to be correct. The cost of being wrong about it dwarfs the cost of being wrong about anything else a government is currently doing. The asymmetry is not theoretical. It is the central fact of the next decade, and most of the institutions that will have to navigate it do not yet know it exists.
