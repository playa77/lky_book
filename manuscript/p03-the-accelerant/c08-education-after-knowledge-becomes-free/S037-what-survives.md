If the argument of the previous two sections is correct — that AGI commoditizes knowledge transfer and credentialing, and that the education system that survives this commoditization is one optimized to produce capacities rather than to transmit content — then the question becomes operational. What specific capacities survive? Which of the things that education currently attempts to produce will still have economic and social value when machines can outperform most university graduates across most cognitive domains? And can those capacities be deliberately cultivated, or are they gifts of temperament and circumstance that formal education cannot reliably produce?

The answer, condensed from the educational research, the cognitive science, and the lived experience of every institution that has ever been required to produce capable people at scale, is that six capacities survive. They are not a comprehensive catalog of human value — nothing that can meaningfully be called human flourishing reduces to a list of six competencies — but they are the capacities that an AGI economy will reward. Education systems that can produce them will produce populations that have something to contribute to an economy that does not need their knowledge. Education systems that cannot produce them will produce populations whose economic irrelevance is guaranteed by the technology itself.

**Judgment** is the capacity to make decisions under conditions of uncertainty, where the available information is incomplete, the relevant variables are unquantifiable, and the consequences of error are significant. It is not the same as knowledge, though it depends on knowledge. It is not the same as intelligence, though it correlates with intelligence. It is the specific capacity to weigh incommensurable considerations — a legal principle against a practical outcome, a short-term cost against a long-term benefit, a rule against an exception that the rule was not designed to handle — and arrive at a decision that can be defended. Machines can process information faster than humans, can identify patterns that humans miss, can generate options that humans would not have considered. They cannot exercise judgment in this sense, because judgment requires the capacity to assign weight to considerations for which no training data exists — to decide, in a genuinely novel situation, what matters and what does not. This capacity is teachable, through structured exposure to complex decisions with real stakes and through the deliberate cultivation of the practice of justification: the requirement that decisions be explained and defended, not merely announced. It is assessable, through the evaluation of decision quality in simulated and real environments. It is, and always has been, the single capacity that distinguishes effective leaders from knowledgeable ones, competent professionals from credentialed ones, and adults from children.

**Agency** is the capacity to initiate action in the absence of external direction. It is the opposite of compliance — not the refusal to follow instructions, but the ability to function when there are no instructions to follow. In an economy in which machines can execute any specified task, the human contribution shifts from execution to identification: deciding what should be done, in what order, toward what end, with what tradeoffs. This requires a capacity that Western progressive education has sometimes claimed to value but has rarely succeeded in cultivating at scale, because genuine agency is uncomfortable to institutional authority. A student who exercises agency asks questions the curriculum does not anticipate, proposes approaches the teacher has not authorized, and pursues interests that do not map neatly onto assessment rubrics. The institutional response, in most systems, is to suppress the agency and reward the compliance — because compliance is easier to manage, easier to assess, and easier to defend to parents, inspectors, and accreditors. The systems that produce agency at scale — and Singapore's, despite its many constraints, is one of them — do so by building into their architecture the expectation that students will be required to act independently, under pressure, with consequences, from an early age. Agency is not a personality trait. It is a capacity that develops when the environment demands it and atrophies when the environment does not.

**Resilience** is the capacity to absorb failure, disappointment, and setback without reduction in function. It is not stoicism — the suppression of emotional response — but the specific ability to experience difficulty and continue operating. The research on resilience converges on a finding that is uncomfortable for the educational philosophy that has dominated Western systems for two generations: resilience is primarily built through exposure to difficulty that is survived, not avoided. The systems that produce resilient people are the systems that do not protect their students from failure, that do not lower standards to preserve self-esteem, that do not treat discomfort as evidence of harm. Singapore's system, with its high-stakes examinations at age twelve, its unapologetic ranking and sorting, and its refusal to pretend that all outcomes are equal, produced rates of psychological stress that were genuinely concerning — and also produced a population whose capacity to function under pressure was the foundation of the country's economic performance. The tradeoff is real, and the question AGI poses is not whether the tradeoff is comfortable but whether societies that have systematically removed failure, disappointment, and setback from the educational experience of their young people have produced populations that can function in an economy that will subject them to all three at unprecedented scale.

**Ethical reasoning** is the capacity to identify the moral dimensions of a decision, to apply principles consistently across contexts, and to recognize when a situation demands the modification of a principle rather than its rigid application. It is not the same as moral sentiment — the feeling that something is right or wrong, which machines can simulate and which humans can experience without ever subjecting to analysis. It is the specific cognitive operation of reasoning from principles to conclusions, testing conclusions against intuitions, and revising either principles or intuitions when they conflict. This capacity is teachable — it has been taught, for centuries, through the structured study of cases, through the practice of argument, and through the discipline of subjecting one's own convictions to adversarial testing. It is assessable — not through multiple-choice tests of moral knowledge but through the evaluation of reasoning quality in response to genuinely difficult cases. And it is indispensable in an AGI economy because the decisions that machines will recommend — about resource allocation, about risk distribution, about which populations bear which costs — will embed moral judgments that the machines themselves cannot justify, because justification requires the capacity to assign weight to incommensurable values, which is precisely the capacity that judgment, and its moral subcomponent, ethical reasoning, provide.

**Physical competence** is the capacity to act effectively in the material world. It is the most neglected competency in contemporary educational discourse, because the dominant assumption of the knowledge economy was that physical competence would be economically irrelevant for the population that education was primarily designed to serve. That assumption was always contestable — the correlation between physical activity and cognitive performance is robust, and the health costs of a sedentary population are large enough to constitute a fiscal emergency in every developed country — but AGI gives it a new dimension. The capacities that machines will commoditize are exclusively cognitive. The capacities that require a human body — dexterity, spatial awareness, endurance, the integration of perception and action in real time — are not commoditized by a technology that operates entirely in silico. Physical competence also serves a function that is not directly economic: it is the most reliable intervention known to psychological science for the maintenance of mental health, the development of discipline, and the cultivation of the specific form of confidence that comes from knowing that one's body can be relied upon. Education systems that treat physical competence as an elective — a concession to the athletically inclined, to be dropped the moment academic pressure intensifies — are producing populations whose economic value is exclusively cognitive, at the moment when exclusively cognitive value is being commoditized.

**Interpersonal depth** is the capacity to form, maintain, and repair relationships characterized by trust, understanding, and mutual obligation. It is the competency that underlies every form of collective human endeavor — from the team that builds a product to the community that raises a child to the polity that governs itself — and it is the competency that Western education has most systematically neglected, because interpersonal depth does not appear on standardized tests and does not translate into the metrics by which schools, teachers, and systems are evaluated. The evidence that it can be cultivated is strong. The interventions that cultivate it — sustained cooperative work toward shared goals, the deliberate teaching of communication and conflict-resolution skills, the creation of environments in which relationships form across lines of difference — are well-understood and demonstrably effective. What is missing is not the knowledge of how to produce interpersonal depth. It is the institutional will to treat it as a primary educational outcome rather than a fortunate byproduct that some students happen to acquire. In an AGI economy, the premium on interpersonal depth will rise for the same reason that the premium on every other non-automatable capacity will rise: because it is scarce, and the forces producing the scarcity are strengthening. A machine can simulate empathy. It cannot experience it, and the simulation will be sufficient for most commercial interactions. But the interactions that determine organizational performance, institutional legitimacy, and social cohesion are not commercial. They depend on the specific human capacity to be known by another person, to be accountable to another person, and to cooperate with another person in the pursuit of ends that neither could achieve alone. A population that has been educated primarily through screens, assessed primarily through algorithms, and socialized primarily through platforms optimized for engagement rather than connection will not possess this capacity at the levels that an AGI economy will demand.

These six capacities are not a curriculum. They are a specification. An education system that produces them will survive the commoditization of knowledge. An education system that does not produce them will educate its population for obsolescence. The question that the following section addresses is not whether the specification is correct — the research consensus, the historical record, and the logic of the argument converge on the same answer — but whether any Western education system is capable of reorganizing itself around it, at the speed that AGI's arrival demands, before the obsolescence is complete.
