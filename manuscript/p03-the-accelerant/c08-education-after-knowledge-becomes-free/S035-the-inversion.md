The most successful education system in the world, measured by the only metric that predicts national outcomes — how well a population can solve novel problems — was designed around a premise that is about to become false. Singapore built its schools on the assumption that knowledge is scarce, that transmitting it from the people who have it to the people who need it is the central function of education, and that the credential that certifies the transmission is the gateway to economic participation. This assumption has been the operating premise of formal education everywhere for two centuries. It is also, as of roughly the present moment, obsolete. The arrival of artificial general intelligence as a production-capable technology does not merely change how education is delivered. It changes what education is for.

The premise is so obvious that it is rarely stated: the value of formal education lies in the transfer of knowledge and the certification of competence. Schools exist to put things into heads that were not previously there. Universities exist to verify that those things are in fact in those heads, and to issue the paperwork that employers, professions, and the state accept as proof. Neither of these functions survives contact with a technology that can produce expert-level knowledge outputs on demand, in real time, at zero marginal cost, across every domain in which formal education currently operates. By 2025, large language models were already passing the United States Medical Licensing Examination, the bar exam in multiple jurisdictions, and graduate-level assessments across the sciences and humanities — not at the threshold of competence but in the top decile of human performance. The trajectory from GPT-3's undergraduate-level writing in 2020 to GPT-4's professional-exam performance in 2023 is not a plateau. It is an ascent whose endpoint is not yet visible. When the answer to any question, the solution to any structured problem, and the production of any document that once required years of training to produce can be generated by a machine in seconds, the economic transaction on which the entire educational enterprise rests — years of tuition and deferred earning in exchange for knowledge that has labor-market value — ceases to make sense. The knowledge is free. The credential is meaningless if what it certifies is something a machine can do better.

This is the inversion. Education has historically been a supply-constrained good. The people who possessed knowledge — teachers, professors, the authors of textbooks — were the bottleneck, and the institutions that aggregated them were the gatekeepers. AGI eliminates both the bottleneck and the gate. The supply of expert-level knowledge production becomes effectively infinite. The question that every education system must now answer is not how to transmit knowledge more efficiently — the improvement that has obsessed reformers for decades, from standardized curricula to digital learning platforms to personalized tutoring algorithms — but what education is supposed to produce when knowledge transfer is no longer its central function.

The answer is not mysterious. It has been available, in various forms, for as long as people have thought seriously about what education should accomplish beyond the transmission of information. The answer is that education must produce people who can do the things that machines, even the most capable machines, cannot do — or at least cannot do in the combination and under the conditions that human life requires. The answer is judgment, not recall. Agency, not compliance. Resilience, not fragility. The capacity to make decisions under uncertainty, to navigate conditions for which no training data exists, to find questions worth asking rather than answers to questions that have already been asked. The answer is not new. It is merely that the arrival of AGI transforms it from a philosophical preference into a survival requirement. For two hundred years, a society could afford to misunderstand what education was for — could treat it as a content-delivery and credentialing pipeline — because the misunderstanding was economically functional. Knowledge was scarce, credentials were legible, and the pipeline produced people who could participate in an economy whose demands they roughly matched. AGI breaks the link. The economy that is arriving will not reward people for possessing knowledge. It will reward people for doing what cannot be automated — and the list of activities that cannot be automated shrinks with every generation of model capability. The education system that will produce those people looks less like the current Western model than it looks like the system LKY built — a system designed, from its founding, to prioritize character formation, discipline, adaptability, and the capacity to act under pressure over the mere accumulation of certified knowledge. That system was not designed for AGI. It was designed for survival. But the requirements of survival in a resource-poor city-state in 1965 turn out to be remarkably similar to the requirements of survival in an AGI economy in 2035.

The speed of this transformation is the variable that makes it a governance problem rather than an interesting seminar topic. Educational reform in Western democracies operates on timelines measured in decades. The adoption of a new curriculum framework takes five to ten years from proposal to implementation. The retraining of a teaching workforce takes a generation. The reform of a university credentialing system that is embedded in labor markets, regulatory frameworks, and social status hierarchies has no precedent for occurring at any speed at all — the modern university's basic architecture of departments, majors, credit hours, and degrees has been stable for more than a century. Meanwhile, the capabilities that are commoditizing knowledge transfer — large language models, multimodal systems, reasoning engines — are improving on timelines measured in months. The conservative estimate — that AGI systems capable of outperforming most university graduates across most cognitive domains will be production-capable by the early 2030s — gives education systems approximately one decade to complete a transformation whose closest historical analogue, the shift from elite clerical education to mass compulsory schooling, took more than a century and required the political energy of industrialization, democratization, and two world wars. The aggressive estimate, which is the one that the people building the technology increasingly believe, gives them half that. In either scenario, the gap between the speed at which the premise of education is being destroyed and the speed at which education systems are capable of changing their premise is not a gap that can be closed by incremental reform. It is a gap that can only be closed by a system that is designed, from its constitutional architecture, to make transformative decisions at speed — or by a crisis so severe that it temporarily overcomes the institutional resistance to transformation that is built into democratic educational governance. The second path is the one that most Western countries are implicitly betting on. It is not a bet that a society designed by LKY would make.

This is the problem that the following sections examine. Whether any Western education system — the institutions that credential, the teachers who deliver, the publics that demand, the unions that protect, the political systems that fund and regulate — can reorganize around a new purpose in less time than it takes to build a single new high school campus. Whether the alternative — an education system built on a different premise, designed by a government that treated the question as an engineering problem rather than a cultural negotiation, and producing the outcomes that an AGI economy will actually reward — can be adapted to institutional environments that lack Singapore's structural advantages. And whether, when knowledge is free, the societies that built their education systems around the transmission of knowledge will discover, too late, that they educated their populations for an economy that no longer exists.
