If LKY had been asked, in his final years, what determined the distribution of power in any society, he would not have begun with theory. He would have begun with a question: who controls the thing everyone else needs? The question was the foundation of his political economy. Land, capital, labor, information — the form of the critical resource changed across centuries, but the logic did not. Control the critical resource, and you control the society that depends on it. Share the critical resource, and you share the society. Lose control of the critical resource, and you lose the capacity to determine your own future. The proposition was not ideological. It was operational. LKY applied it to water, to port access, to military technology, to the talent pipeline that filled his cabinet. In every case, the question was the same: who controls it, and what follows from that answer?

Apply the question to artificial general intelligence and the answer is not ambiguous. In early 2026, the frontier of AGI development — the systems most likely to become the substrate on which future economic, military, and governmental activity runs — is controlled by a small and shrinking number of organizations. The consolidation has been rapid. In 2023, approximately a dozen organizations worldwide possessed the capital, the talent, and the compute infrastructure to train frontier models. By 2025, that number had contracted to roughly five. The leading American labs together with one or two Chinese competitors command the overwhelming majority of frontier training capacity. Everyone else is running behind a frontier that is moving too fast to catch, on hardware they cannot afford and with talent they cannot retain. The structure is not an industry. It is an oligopoly consolidating toward a duopoly, and the gravitational center of that consolidation is a handful of corporate boardrooms in San Francisco and a handful of state-directed laboratories in Beijing. The rest of the world is not in the race. It is watching the race, and its position in the outcome will be determined by whoever finishes first.

The political implications follow directly from the structure. Whoever controls the frontier controls not only the technology but the terms on which everyone else accesses it. This is not a prediction. It is a description of how oligopolies work — in oil, in semiconductors, in shipping, in every domain where a critical resource is concentrated in a small number of hands. The companies that build AGI will not only sell it. They will shape the standards on which it runs, the regulatory frameworks in which it operates, the protocols by which it integrates with existing systems, and the prices at which it is made available to governments, businesses, and individuals. They will do this not because they are malevolent but because they are corporations, and corporations exist to maximize the returns to their owners within the legal and competitive constraints that define their operating environment. The constraints currently governing frontier AI development are, as the previous section established, effectively absent. The owners of AGI will therefore set the terms. Everyone else will accept them or do without. Doing without, in an AGI-saturated economy, is not a viable competitive strategy.

LKY would have recognized this structure instantly, because he spent his career making certain that Singapore was never on the wrong side of it. His water policy is the clearest example. For four decades, Singapore depended on water treaties with Malaysia — treaties that would eventually expire, treaties whose renewal was subject to the political calculations of a larger neighbor. LKY recognized that dependence on a critical resource controlled by someone else was not a bargaining position. It was a vulnerability. He invested over decades, at extraordinary expense, against the advice of every international consultant, in achieving water self-sufficiency. The investment was not made because desalination was cheaper. It was made because the treaty price was set in Kuala Lumpur, not in Singapore, and the cost of vulnerability exceeded the cost of the plants. The principle generalizes. A society that depends on a critical resource controlled by someone else is a tenant in its own country. The owner decides. The tenant adapts.

The distributional implications of AGI extend beyond sovereignty into the structure of economic life. The technology will substitute for human labor across an expanding range of cognitive tasks — legal analysis, software engineering, medical diagnosis, strategic planning, creative production. The displaced worker bears the cost. The technology's owner captures the productivity gain. The dynamic is not new. It has governed every wave of automation. The difference is the scale and speed. Previous waves displaced physical labor and created cognitive employment. AGI displaces cognitive labor, and it is not clear what comes next. The displaced worker receives whatever the political settlement provides — retraining programs for jobs the technology is also displacing, benefits designed for a labor market that no longer exists, or nothing. This is not dystopia. It is the default distributional outcome of concentrated capital ownership in a market economy, and it will hold unless specific institutional mechanisms are designed to prevent it.

The mechanisms exist, and LKY's Singapore deployed several of them. The Central Provident Fund ensured that every worker owned a stake in the national economy — compulsory savings that financed infrastructure, housing, healthcare, and retirement. The Housing and Development Board ensured that the single largest asset most families possessed — their home — appreciated as the country prospered. Sovereign wealth funds captured the returns from high-growth decades and reinvested them in the future. These were not redistributive mechanisms in the Western sense. They were pre-distributive mechanisms — structures that ensured the benefits of growth were distributed widely before they concentrated, rather than taxed after they had already concentrated. The distinction was essential to LKY's thinking. Redistribution fights the outcome. Pre-distribution designs the baseline.

AGI makes pre-distribution urgent for a reason that capital ownership in previous technological eras did not. Capital, in the form of factories, machines, and infrastructure, is inherently distributed — a factory is in a place, employs people in that place, and generates economic activity in that place. AGI is not. It runs in data centers, serves users through the internet, and concentrates its returns in the jurisdiction where its owners happen to be incorporated. A government that wants to ensure broad distribution of the benefits of a factory can require the factory to be in the country and employ its citizens. A government that wants to ensure broad distribution of the benefits of an AGI system has no similar leverage, because the system does not need to be in the country, does not need to employ its citizens, and can be moved — or licensed, or accessed — from anywhere with an internet connection and a legal framework the company finds acceptable. The mobility of capital has always constrained the bargaining power of governments. The mobility of intelligence, when that intelligence is digital and owned, removes the constraint entirely. The government that wants a share of the returns must negotiate from a position it has never occupied before: the position of a supplicant asking for access to a resource it cannot create, cannot control, and cannot directly tax.

This is the structural context in which the governance question must be asked. It is not a question of fairness. LKY never asked whether a situation was fair. He asked whether it was sustainable. A society in which a small number of corporations control the most powerful cognitive technology ever invented, capture the vast majority of its economic returns, and operate under regulatory frameworks that are, by their own architects' admission, inadequate — that society is not facing a fairness problem. It is facing a stability problem. The evidence from every previous era of concentrated technological power — the Gilded Age, the industrial monopolies, the financialization of the late twentieth century — suggests that the answer is no, not without major institutional reform. The difference in the AGI era is that the technology that concentrates power also eliminates the bargaining power that previous generations used to demand reform. Workers organized unions from a position of structural necessity — the economy needed their labor. Voters demanded redistribution from a position of structural necessity — the political system needed their votes. AGI weakens both forms of leverage simultaneously.

LKY's answer to this predicament would not have been to moralize about corporate power. He would have asked what institutional mechanisms could be built — before the concentration became irreversible — to ensure that the benefits of the technology were distributed broadly enough to sustain the social compact that makes governance possible. The mechanisms he built in Singapore were specific to Singapore's circumstances, but the principle behind them was general: do not wait for concentration to produce crisis, because crisis produces overreaction, and overreaction produces outcomes worse than either the concentration or the reform. Build the distributional mechanism before the distributional problem arrives. The question the West now faces is whether it retains the institutional capacity to do the same, or whether the forces that produced the concentration will also prevent the correction. The answer to that question depends on whether the institutions that would need to build those mechanisms are capable of doing so. It depends, in short, on competence.
