The competence gap is not evenly distributed. Some political systems are better at speed than others. Some are better at technical recruitment. Some plan further ahead than the electoral cycle permits. The question is not whether any system is perfectly configured for AGI governance — none is — but whether any of the major approaches currently on offer is closing the gap, and whether the gap is even closable under the institutional constraints that define each approach. The analysis that follows applies the three-demand framework developed in the previous section — speed, technical competence, long-term planning — to the three governing models that will determine the future of the technology: the American, the Chinese, and the European. It then asks whether a fourth model, the Singaporean, demonstrates capacities that the other three lack, and what follows if it does.

The American approach to AGI governance is, at this moment, an approach in name only. The United States possesses the world's leading AI companies, the largest concentration of frontier research talent, and the most advanced computing infrastructure. It does not possess a single piece of binding federal legislation governing artificial intelligence. The executive order issued in 2023 was a statement of intent with voluntary compliance mechanisms. Congressional hearings have been held, white papers published, bipartisan commissions proposed. No law has been enacted. The speed dimension, measured against the operational tempo of the technology, registers as a comprehensive failure. The reasons are not mysterious. They are the same reasons the United States cannot pass budgets on time or address the trajectory of the national debt. The institutional architecture is designed for deliberation and veto, not for speed, and the mismatch is structural.

The technical competence dimension is equally unfavorable. The talent pool exists — the United States produces and attracts more AI researchers than any other country — but it exists almost entirely in the private sector. Government agencies cannot compete for it, for the reasons the previous section detailed: pay scales, working conditions, and institutional cultures that are actively hostile to the kind of people who build things. The result is that the institutions assigned to regulate the technology do not understand it well enough to regulate it effectively, and the informational asymmetry is so total that the regulated parties effectively set the terms of their own regulation. This is not a conspiracy. It is a market outcome, and it is the default condition of every domain in which the government regulates an industry it does not understand and cannot attract the talent to understand. The long-term planning dimension is perhaps the most damning. The American political system is organized around two-year, four-year, and six-year cycles. AGI governance requires planning horizons measured in decades. The two timescales are incompatible, and the election cycle, being the shorter of the two, determines every outcome that requires political decision-making to achieve. The United States cannot plan for AGI because its political system is not designed to plan for anything that takes longer than the interval between elections.

The result, viewed through LKY's framework, is a paradox. The country that is building the technology is structurally incapable of governing it. The country that has the most to gain from effective AGI governance — because its companies stand to capture the largest share of the economic returns, and because its military depends on technological superiority — is the country whose institutions are least capable of delivering it. LKY, who spent his career observing American power and diagnosing American weakness, would not have found this surprising. He would have found it consistent with every pattern he had been documenting for forty years. The American system is extraordinarily good at generating technological innovation and extraordinarily bad at governing its consequences. The two capabilities were never the same capability, and the gap between them is not being closed. It is being widened by the very forces that produced the innovation in the first place.

The Chinese approach is in many respects the mirror image of the American. Where the United States cannot move quickly, China can — not because its institutions are more efficient in any technical sense but because the structure of political authority is different. When the Chinese Communist Party decides that a technology is strategically important, resources are allocated, regulations are written, and implementation begins on a timeline that democratic systems cannot match. The speed dimension is the one on which China performs best, and the evidence from its AI strategy documents, its national laboratory investments, and its domestic regulatory framework suggests that the pattern will hold. The party-state can move fast, and on AGI governance, it is moving fast.

The technical competence dimension is more complex. China is investing heavily in AI research and has produced frontier models that compete with the best American systems. Its talent pipeline is deep and growing. But the question that LKY would ask is not whether China has technical competence at the level of its leading labs. It is whether the institutions assigned to govern the technology can exercise independent technical judgment over the entities building it, or whether those entities — many of which are state-directed or state-adjacent — effectively govern themselves. The distinction between the builder and the regulator, which is already blurred in China's state-capitalist model, becomes invisible when both report to the same political authority. A system in which the regulator and the regulated are accountable to the same party committee is a system in which governance is not independent. It is administration, and the difference between the two is the difference between a regulator that can say no and a regulator that can only say yes, faster.

The long-term planning dimension is China's strongest on paper and its most dangerous in practice. The Chinese party-state plans in decades — its industrial policy, its demographic and energy strategies are structured around timelines that Western governments cannot match. LKY himself regarded this capacity as the Chinese system's clearest advantage over democratic alternatives. His assessment was operational, not ideological. A system that plans for fifty years and executes has an advantage over a system that plans for four. But the advantage carries a structural risk that the planning horizon obscures. A system that makes no provision for error-correction — no independent judiciary, no free press, no competitive elections, no mechanism for reversing a bad decision once made at the highest level — is a system in which the cost of being wrong is catastrophically high. When the party-state decides correctly, it executes faster than any democratic competitor. When it decides incorrectly — and on a technology as complex as AGI, incorrect decisions are unavoidable — the consequences are distributed across 1.4 billion people with no electoral mechanism for demanding correction.

The European approach is the most legally sophisticated and the least operationally capable. The European Union has produced the world's most comprehensive regulatory frameworks for digital technology — the General Data Protection Regulation, the Digital Services Act, the AI Act — and each of them reflects a seriousness of legislative purpose that neither the United States nor China can match. The problem is that legislative purpose and operational capacity are different things, and the gap between them defines the European approach to AGI governance. The speed dimension is the most obvious failure. The GDPR took six years from proposal to effectiveness. The AI Act's transition periods extend to 2027. The technology being regulated in 2027 will not be the technology the legislation was designed for in 2021, because the pace of AI development is not synchronized with the pace of European legislative deliberation. The EU writes thorough rules for technologies that have already been replaced by the time the rules take effect. The pattern is not a failure of ambition. It is a failure of institutional design. A system that requires twenty-seven member states, multiple parliamentary readings, and years of negotiation to produce a single regulatory framework cannot govern a technology that advances in months. The institutional machinery is not designed for this pace, and it cannot be redesigned without abandoning the consensus-based legitimacy that is the foundation of the European project.

The technical competence dimension is equally challenging, though for different reasons. Europe produces excellent AI researchers and has world-class academic institutions. But it does not produce frontier AI companies. The talent exists, but it migrates to American laboratories and, increasingly, to Chinese ones, because that is where the frontier is. The regulatory apparatus, meanwhile, is staffed by lawyers and policy specialists who understand the law but do not, as a rule, understand the technology. The EU's AI Office, established to oversee the AI Act's implementation, was, at its founding, staffed primarily by policy generalists rather than technical specialists. The informational asymmetry that defines the American regulatory context defines the European one as well, with the additional complication that the European regulator cannot even observe the technology it is supposed to regulate from the inside. There is no European frontier lab whose internal operations can provide a window into the technology's trajectory. The regulator is regulating from the outside, without independent technical capacity, and the result is regulation that is legally elaborate and practically toothless.

The long-term planning dimension is where Europe performs best among the three, though the baseline is low. The European project is, in its conception, a long-term undertaking — the gradual construction of a supranational political entity through institutional accretion rather than revolutionary rupture. The planning horizon extends beyond election cycles in ways that the American system's does not, because the European institutions are designed, in part, to be insulated from the electoral pressures that produce short-termism in national governments. But the insulation comes at the cost of the speed deficit already described, and the result is a planning capacity that understands what needs to be done but cannot act quickly enough to do it. Europe can see the future more clearly than the United States and can plan for it more coherently than China, but it cannot execute the plan, and the inability to execute renders the clarity academic.

Apply LKY's framework to all three approaches and the pattern is consistent. Each major governing model possesses one of the three capacities AGI governance demands and lacks the other two. The United States possesses the technology but lacks the governance. China possesses the speed and the planning horizon but lacks the institutional counterweights that prevent catastrophic error. Europe possesses the regulatory seriousness but lacks the speed and the technical capacity to enforce what it legislates. None of the three is closing the competence gap, because each is structurally prevented from developing the capacities it lacks. The gap, as the previous chapter established, is a system of mutually reinforcing failures — and the systems that would need to close it are themselves the products of those failures.

Singapore is the anomaly. The city-state possesses, at its scale, all three capacities that the major powers lack. Its government can move quickly — not because it is authoritarian in the Chinese sense but because its single-chamber parliament, its dominant-party continuity, and its institutional culture of technical decision-making eliminate the veto points that paralyze American and European governance. It recruits technical talent into government by paying competitive salaries and maintaining an institutional culture that values engineering competence over political loyalty — the "peanuts and monkeys" principle institutionalized as a governing philosophy rather than a slogan. It plans in fifty-year arcs, not because its leaders are unusually farsighted but because the system was deliberately designed to insulate long-term decisions from short-term political pressure. The capacities that AGI governance demands — speed, technical competence, long-term planning — are the capacities that the Singaporean system was built to produce. They are not accidents of history or personality. They are design features of an institutional architecture that was constructed, brick by brick, over six decades, by a leader who understood that institutions determine outcomes and that the quality of institutions is never an accident.

The question Singapore poses is not whether its model is replicable at the scale of the continental powers. The question is whether the principles that produced its capacities can be extracted and applied in different institutional contexts, or whether the capacities are inseparable from the specific conditions — existential crisis, small population, single dominant party, a founding leader of unusual ability and unusual duration — that made Singapore possible. If the principles are separable from the conditions, then the competence gap is a design problem with a design solution. If they are not, then AGI governance at the scale of the major powers is, as LKY might have put it, a problem without a precedent, and the relevant question shifts from "how do we close the gap" to "what follows from the fact that it cannot be closed."
