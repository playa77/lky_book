In 2013, the United States government launched a website to enroll citizens in health insurance. The site cost roughly two billion dollars to build and repair. On its first day, fewer than ten people successfully enrolled. The technology involved was well understood, the requirements were stable, and the problem — connecting citizens to a database of insurance plans — was one that private companies had been solving at scale for more than a decade. The government could not do it. A decade later, the same government's most senior elected officials were being asked to regulate artificial intelligence systems whose internal operations were opaque even to their creators, whose capabilities were advancing on a trajectory no one could reliably predict, and whose economic and military implications exceeded those of any technology introduced in the preceding century. The expectation that the same set of institutions could rise to this challenge was not grounded in evidence. It was grounded in the assumption that when the stakes are high enough, institutions find a way. LKY spent his career demonstrating that this assumption was wrong. When the stakes are high enough, institutions do what they are built to do. If they are not built to do the thing being asked of them, they fail, and the stakes do not change the outcome. They only change the cost of the failure.

The capabilities that AGI governance demands can be grouped into three categories, and each category must be assessed against the evidence of what Western governments actually deliver, not against what their constitutions promise or their leaders' speeches describe. The assessment that follows is not a forecast. It is an audit of existing capacity, conducted in the present tense, using data that is publicly available and outcomes that are already on the record. The question it answers is not whether Western governments will rise to the occasion. It is whether they possess, at this moment, the institutional machinery to do so. The answer, across all three dimensions, is that they do not.

The first demand is speed. AGI systems already operate on timescales that compress decision-making from years to hours, and the compression is accelerating. A frontier model can be trained, tested, and deployed in months. A regulatory response that takes years arrives to govern a technology that is no longer the technology being used. This is not a hypothetical scenario. It is the history of every regulatory framework applied to digital technology in the past three decades. The European Union's General Data Protection Regulation was proposed in 2012 and took effect in 2018. By the time enforcement began in earnest, the technology landscape it was designed to govern — the social media platforms, the data brokers, the advertising infrastructure — had been transformed twice over. The EU's AI Act, passed in 2024 after multiple years of negotiation, contains transition periods that extend to 2027. The technology it was designed to regulate in 2021, when the legislative process began, is not the technology that will exist when the transition periods expire. The pace of the legislative process and the pace of the technological process are not merely different. They are incomparable. One moves at the speed of democratic deliberation. The other moves at the speed of competition between the best-capitalized technology companies in history. The result is not a lag. It is a structural incapacity to close the gap, and the gap is widening.

The problem is not limited to legislation. It extends into the operational capacity of the regulatory agencies that would enforce whatever laws are eventually passed. The agencies are staffed, in most Western countries, by career civil servants whose hiring, promotion, and retention are governed by pay scales and working conditions that are not competitive with the private sector they are supposed to regulate. The result is predictable. In 2024, the United States Department of Justice's antitrust division had fewer than eight hundred employees. The legal departments of the technology companies it was investigating employed tens of thousands. The Federal Trade Commission's entire technology enforcement budget was a rounding error in the capital expenditure reports of the companies whose conduct it was supposed to police. None of this is new. None of it is secret. None of it is being addressed at a scale that suggests anyone in a position of authority takes it seriously. The institutions that are supposed to govern the technology are outnumbered, outspent, and outmatched by the institutions that build it, and the ratio is not improving.

The second demand is technical competence. Effective regulation of AGI requires regulators who can understand the technology well enough to distinguish between credible safety claims and public relations, between genuine capability advances and marketing, between alignment techniques that actually constrain behavior and alignment techniques that produce the appearance of constraint while leaving the underlying dynamics unchanged. The current regulatory workforce in every Western country lacks this competence, and there is no pipeline that will supply it. The reason is not a mystery. A software engineer or machine learning researcher who is good enough to evaluate frontier AI systems can earn, in the private sector, three to ten times what any government agency can offer. The career path that leads from a technical PhD to a regulatory agency does not exist, because the agencies have not built it and have shown no serious interest in building it. The people who understand the technology work for the companies that build it. The people who are supposed to regulate the technology do not understand it. The informational asymmetry is total, and it is produced by the same market dynamics that produced the technology itself. The companies attract the talent because they can pay for it. The governments cannot attract the talent because they cannot pay for it. The governments therefore cannot evaluate the claims the companies make. The companies therefore make whatever claims they wish, and the regulatory process becomes a negotiation in which one party possesses all of the relevant information and the other party does not know enough to know what it does not know.

This is the structural context that LKY's "peanuts and monkeys" dictum was designed to prevent. He insisted that Singapore's government pay competitive salaries and recruit from the top of the talent pool not because he believed in generous compensation as a matter of principle but because he understood that the alternative was an informational asymmetry that would make effective governance impossible. A government that cannot attract competent people cannot govern competently. A government that cannot govern competently cannot govern a technology as powerful as AGI. The logic is linear and, in his framework, inescapable. The West has chosen a different path — not through any explicit decision but through decades of political choices that made government service less attractive, less prestigious, and less well-compensated relative to the private sector — and that path leads directly to the regulatory incapacity that AGI governance most urgently requires not to exist.

The third demand is long-term planning. AGI governance requires institutions that can make decisions whose consequences will unfold over decades and whose costs will be borne by generations that cannot vote in the elections that determine whether the decisions are made. This requirement collides directly with the architecture of democratic accountability. An elected official whose re-election depends on conditions in the next two to four years has no incentive to invest resources in preventing a crisis that will materialize in ten to twenty. The voters who would reward the investment do not yet exist. The voters who exist are, by the logic of the electoral system, more concerned with the immediate and the visible than with the distant and the abstract. The result is that long-term problems are systematically deferred until they become short-term crises, at which point the options for addressing them are worse than the options that were available when deferral began. Climate change, pension obligations, infrastructure maintenance, public debt — the pattern repeats across every domain where the costs of inaction are deferred and the costs of action are immediate. AGI fits the pattern exactly, with the additional feature that the technology that demands long-term planning also compresses the time available to do it.

LKY's fifty-year planning horizon was not a personality trait. It was an institutional design choice. The Singaporean system was structured to insulate long-term decisions from short-term political pressure — through mechanisms that are not replicable in democratic systems with competitive elections and free presses and the accountability structures that those systems require. The question is not whether Western democracies should adopt Singapore's planning horizon. They cannot. The mechanisms that enable it require tradeoffs that democratic publics, by and large, are not willing to make. The question is whether Western democracies can develop alternative mechanisms that produce a planning horizon long enough to govern a technology whose consequences will span generations, using institutions whose planning horizon, by design, spans election cycles. The answer, at present, is that they have not.

The competence gap is not a single problem with a single solution. It is three problems — speed, technical capacity, and planning horizon — that interact with each other in ways that make each individually harder to solve. The speed problem prevents the technical capacity problem from being addressed, because building technical capacity takes time that the technology's pace does not permit. The technical capacity problem prevents the planning horizon problem from being addressed, because institutions that do not understand the technology cannot plan for its consequences. The planning horizon problem prevents the speed problem from being addressed, because institutions with short time horizons cannot invest in the long-term reforms that would accelerate their own decision-making. The three failures form a closed loop, each reinforcing the others, and the loop is not self-correcting. The corrector's paradox — the system's correctors are its products, and the system produces people who replicate the system — applies here with particular force. The institutions that would need to close the competence gap are the same institutions that the competence gap describes. They cannot fix themselves, because the self that would do the fixing is the self that is broken.

This is the structural analysis that LKY would have offered. He would not have offered it as an indictment. He would have offered it as a diagnosis, because in his framework, a diagnosis is the prerequisite for a prescription. The question that follows from the diagnosis is whether any of the major approaches to AGI governance currently on offer — the American, the Chinese, the European — addresses the competence gap at the scale and speed the technology demands. Whether any of them can close it is the question to which the analysis now turns.
