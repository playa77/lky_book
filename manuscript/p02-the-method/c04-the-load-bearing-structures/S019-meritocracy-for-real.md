Every modern society claims to be a meritocracy. The claim is near-universal because its alternative — that a society distributes opportunity on the basis of birth, wealth, or connections — is indefensible in public. No political leader in any developed democracy stands for office on a platform of inherited privilege. Every university admissions office, every corporate hiring policy, every government appointment process describes itself as merit-based. The word has been stretched to cover so many different institutional arrangements that it has ceased to mean anything precise. When everything is meritocracy, nothing is, and the most useful service a definition can perform is to distinguish between the genuine article and the brand version that has replaced it in most Western societies.

LKY's definition of meritocracy was operational, not philosophical. A meritocratic system, in his formulation, was one that identified talent wherever it appeared, developed it to its maximum potential, and deployed it in the positions where it could produce the greatest public benefit. The definition had four components, and each was essential. Identification required mechanisms that could find ability in populations where ability was not visible — children from poor families, from non-English-speaking households, from neighborhoods that had never produced a university graduate. Development required institutions that could take raw ability and convert it into usable competence, and that meant a school system organized around rigorous standards rather than self-esteem, examinations that measured performance objectively rather than subjectively, and a culture that treated academic achievement as a public good rather than a private asset. Deployment required a labor market and a public sector that allocated positions on the basis of demonstrated competence rather than credentials, connections, or seniority. And benefit — the fourth component, and the one most often omitted from Western formulations of meritocracy — required that the talent the system identified, developed, and deployed be directed toward the collective welfare rather than the private accumulation of the talented. A system that identified the brightest children from every background, gave them the best education money could buy, and then sent them into investment banking, management consulting, and corporate law was not a meritocracy in LKY's sense. It was an efficient mechanism for converting public investment in talent into private returns for the talented, and the society that invested in them received nothing in return except the satisfaction of knowing that its most capable citizens were doing very well for themselves.

Singapore's meritocratic machinery was built into the education system and the public service. The Primary School Leaving Examination, administered at age twelve, determined which secondary school track a student would enter — a decision that effectively determined whether the student would proceed to university, to a polytechnic, or to a vocational pathway. The system was unforgiving, and it was deliberately so. LKY's view was that the alternative — pretending that all students were equally capable, delaying the moment of differentiation until it was too late to do anything about it, or refusing to differentiate at all — was crueler than the examination system, because it sentenced capable students from disadvantaged backgrounds to an education that would not develop their potential and sentenced less capable students to years of failure and frustration that no amount of self-esteem pedagogy could remedy. The examination was not intended to be comfortable. It was intended to be accurate, and the accuracy was what made it fair. A child from a poor family who scored well on the PSLE would enter the Express stream, receive the most demanding curriculum the country offered, and, if the performance was sustained, receive a government scholarship that would pay for university education in Singapore or abroad — the Public Service Commission scholarship, the most prestigious, or one of the ministry-level scholarships that funded study in specific fields. The child's parents' income, connections, and social status were irrelevant to this process. The only thing that mattered was the examination score, and the examination was designed to measure what the child could do, not what the child's background had prepared them to do. The system was not blind to the advantages that wealthy families conferred on their children — private tutoring, enriched home environments, networks of information and opportunity — but it was designed to be as resistant to those advantages as an examination-based system could be, and it succeeded to a degree that is difficult to replicate without the institutional coherence and political will that made it possible.

The Western version of meritocracy, in LKY's analysis, was a branding exercise. The term was invoked to describe systems in which the children of the wealthy and well-connected attended the best schools — which were often private, and therefore inaccessible to families without the means to pay for them — and then entered the best universities, which were also the most expensive, and then entered the best professions, where their parents' networks were most valuable. The meritocratic claim rested on the fact that the children in question were genuinely talented — they had worked hard, they had performed well on the metrics that mattered, they deserved their positions by any individual measure of merit. But the system that had produced them was not meritocratic in LKY's sense, because it had not identified talent wherever it appeared. It had identified talent where it was already visible, which meant talent that had been cultivated by families with the resources to cultivate it, and it had then credentialed that talent and deployed it in positions that maximized private returns. The result was a society that was highly competent at its upper levels and increasingly incompetent at its middle and lower levels, because the institutions that had once developed talent across the full range of the population — public schools, vocational training programs, apprenticeships, the military, the civil service — had been allowed to atrophy while the institutions that served the already-advantaged had flourished. The society was not a meritocracy. It was an aristocracy of the credentialed, and it was less meritocratic than the systems it had replaced because it provided a legitimizing ideology — "you earned this" — that made the inequalities it produced seem natural rather than structural.

The failure mode of genuine meritocracy, which LKY acknowledged explicitly in the last decade of his life, was that it tends to produce a new hereditary class. The children of the meritocratic elite inherit not only their parents' genes — whatever contribution those make — but their parents' resources, networks, habits, expectations, and access to the supplementary education that makes the difference between a good examination score and an exceptional one. The meritocratic machinery that LKY built was designed to identify talent from disadvantaged backgrounds, but the machinery was fighting against a current that grew stronger with every generation of success. The scholarship holder from a poor family becomes a wealthy professional. The wealthy professional marries another wealthy professional. Their children grow up in a household with books, with educated conversation, with private tutoring, with the thousand invisible advantages that wealth and education confer. By the third generation, the grandchildren of the original scholarship holder are competing for places in the same meritocratic machinery from a starting position so advantaged that their success is almost guaranteed, and the machinery that was designed to find talent in poor families finds it increasingly in rich ones. The system does not stop working. It continues to identify talent and develop it and deploy it. But the talent it identifies is increasingly the talent of the already-advantaged, and the meritocracy becomes, gradually and without anyone intending it, a mechanism for the reproduction of a new elite.

LKY's response to this problem was characteristic. He did not deny it. He did not sentimentalize it. He identified it as a structural problem requiring structural solutions, and he began, in the final years of his public life, to advocate for interventions that would counteract the drift toward hereditary advantage — more aggressive outreach to disadvantaged communities, scholarship programs targeted at families without a history of educational attainment, reforms to the primary school system that would reduce the advantage conferred by expensive preschool education. The interventions were modest, and he acknowledged that they were. The problem was not one that could be solved by a single policy or a single generation of reform. It was a permanent structural tension built into the logic of meritocracy itself, and it required permanent institutional vigilance to prevent the meritocracy from hardening into the kind of class system it was designed to replace. The societies that have adopted the brand version of meritocracy do not have this problem, because they have never had the genuine article to begin with. The societies that have built genuine meritocratic institutions, and Singapore is the clearest example, discover that the institutions require constant maintenance, that the maintenance is politically difficult, and that the alternative — allowing the institutions to decay — is the restoration of hereditary privilege under a meritocratic name. The choice between the two is the choice between a society that makes good on its promises and a society that has learned to be comfortable with breaking them.
