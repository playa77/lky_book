Among the things that Western leaders are not permitted to say in public, the most consequential is that cultural values predict group outcomes. The prohibition is not legal. It is enforced by a professional and media class that treats any attribution of differential outcomes to culture as a form of racism — and in some formulations, as racism's most insidious form, because it appears to operate through neutral language while reaching the same conclusions. A politician who observes that children from families that prioritize education, defer gratification, and maintain two-parent households tend to perform better in school than children from families that do not will be accused of blaming the disadvantaged for their own disadvantage, of ignoring structural barriers, and of providing cover for the withdrawal of public resources from the communities that need them most. Each of these accusations contains a partial truth. The structural barriers are real. The withdrawal of public resources is a genuine danger. And a statement of fact about group-level patterns can easily become a justification for individual-level indifference. But the partial truths do not add up to a complete truth, and the complete truth is that culture — the complex of values, habits, expectations, and norms that a community transmits across generations — is one of the most powerful predictors of outcomes in every domain that matters for human flourishing, and a society that forbids itself from acknowledging this is a society that has disabled one of its most important instruments of understanding and intervention.

LKY's position on this question was unambiguous, and it was the position that made him most unacceptable to Western intellectual opinion during his lifetime. He believed that the differential success of ethnic groups — in Singapore, in Southeast Asia, in the West — was partly explicable by cultural differences, and he believed that the refusal to discuss these differences was not a form of anti-racism but a form of intellectual cowardice that prevented societies from addressing the real sources of inequality. The Chinese in Southeast Asia, he observed, had arrived in most cases as impoverished laborers and had, within a few generations, achieved economic outcomes substantially above the regional average. The same pattern was visible among Chinese diaspora communities in the West, among Korean and Vietnamese immigrants to the United States, and among Indian communities in East Africa and the Caribbean. The pattern was not universal — many individuals and some subcommunities did not follow it — but it was too consistent across countries, continents, and historical periods to be explained by structural advantage alone, since the populations in question had in most cases arrived with nothing, faced discrimination, and lacked political power. LKY's explanation was cultural: the values these communities transmitted — the expectation that children would surpass their parents, the willingness to defer consumption for investment in education, the stability of the family as an economic and social unit, the belief that effort rather than circumstance determined outcomes — produced behaviors that produced results, and the results accumulated across generations.

The distinction that LKY drew, and that his Western critics persistently refused to acknowledge, was between culture and race. Culture, in his understanding, was learned, transmitted, and changeable. A community could acquire new cultural values; it could lose old ones; it could, through deliberate institutional intervention, be steered toward behaviors that produced better outcomes. Race, by contrast, was fixed — a fact of biology that no policy could alter and that no serious person would propose as the basis for social policy. The confusion of the two categories — the assumption that any attribution of group differences to culture was a covert attribution to race — was, in LKY's view, a category error that made it impossible to conduct an honest conversation about why some groups succeeded and others did not, and what could be done to help those who were not succeeding. The error had political origins. In the American context, where the debate was most intense and most influential globally, the history of scientific racism — the long and bloody tradition of attributing black disadvantage to biological inferiority — had made the entire question of group differences radioactive. Any argument that began with "group X does better than group Y because..." was immediately suspected of ending with "...of genetic inferiority," and the suspicion was not unreasonable, given the history. But the solution to the history was not to forbid the conversation. It was to insist on the distinction between culture and race with sufficient clarity and force that the conversation could proceed without the suspicion, and to design institutions that could act on the cultural variables without ever straying into the biological ones. LKY's entire career was a demonstration that this distinction was operational rather than merely philosophical. The Singapore he built was a multiracial society that refused racial determinism — Chinese, Malays, and Indians were held to the same standards, given the same opportunities, and expected to produce the same outcomes — while simultaneously insisting that culture mattered, that some cultural configurations were more conducive to success than others, and that the state had both the right and the responsibility to encourage the conducive configurations and discourage the destructive ones.

The evidence that culture matters is not confined to the Asian success stories that LKY cited. The evidence is visible in every Western country, and it is strongest precisely where the Western consensus is most committed to denying it. In the United States, the children of Nigerian immigrants — a population that is, by American racial categories, black — perform at levels comparable to or exceeding those of white and Asian students, with educational attainment rates, household incomes, and professional representation that are dramatically higher than those of native-born black Americans. The difference cannot be attributed to genetics, since both populations are classified as black. It cannot be attributed primarily to discrimination, since Nigerian immigrants face the same anti-black racism that native-born black Americans face, and in some cases face it without the protection of established community networks. The difference is substantially cultural: the Nigerian immigrant community transmits a set of expectations about education, deferred gratification, and professional achievement that produce different behaviors, which produce different outcomes. The same pattern is visible among Indian immigrants, among Korean immigrants, among Vietnamese refugees — populations that arrived in the West with minimal material resources and substantial cultural capital, and that converted the cultural capital into material outcomes within one or two generations. The pattern is also visible in reverse: the children of wealthy, native-born Western families who attend the best schools and have every structural advantage but who, because of cultural norms that devalue academic effort, that celebrate immediate gratification over deferred consumption, and that treat success as a function of talent rather than work, produce outcomes below what their structural advantages would predict.

The cost of the taboo on cultural explanation is not merely intellectual. It is practical, and it falls most heavily on the populations the taboo is intended to protect. A society that attributes all group disparities to structural factors — to discrimination, to underfunding, to historical injustice — will direct all its remedial efforts toward structural change. It will pass anti-discrimination laws, increase funding for disadvantaged schools, and implement affirmative action programs. Each of these interventions has value, and each addresses a genuine component of the problem. But none of them addresses the cultural component, because the cultural component is the one the society has forbidden itself from naming. The result is a set of policies that attack the problem from every direction except the one that might make the largest difference, and a political discourse in which the people who are most disadvantaged by the taboo are the people on whose behalf it is maintained. A child raised in a family that does not value education, in a community where academic success is socially penalized, in a culture that treats effort as a sign of subservience rather than agency, will not be saved by increased school funding or anti-discrimination laws. The interventions cannot reach the space where the damage is done, and the damage is done in the transmission of values from parents to children, from peers to peers, from the ambient culture to the developing mind. The society that cannot say this out loud is a society that has abandoned the children it claims to protect, and it has abandoned them in the name of a sensitivity that serves the sensibilities of the professional class that enforces it rather than the interests of the people it is supposed to serve.

LKY's position on this question was never popular in the West, and it is not popular now. But it has the property, as many of his positions did, of being testable against outcomes. The test is whether the populations that adopt the cultural values LKY identified as conducive to success — the emphasis on education, the stability of the family, the willingness to defer gratification, the belief that effort determines outcomes — actually achieve better outcomes than populations that do not. The evidence from every country and every historical period in which the question has been examined is that they do. The test of the Western position — that culture is irrelevant, that all group disparities are structural, that acknowledging cultural differences is racism — is whether the populations on whose behalf this position is maintained actually benefit from its maintenance. The evidence from every Western country in which the position has been official policy for the past half-century is that they do not. The gap between the two positions is the gap between what works and what feels virtuous, and it is the same gap that appears in every domain where LKY's pragmatism confronted Western idealism. The difference is that in this domain, the consequences of choosing what feels virtuous over what works are borne by the people who can least afford to bear them.
