In 2015, the year Lee Kuan Yew died, Singapore's fifteen-year-olds ranked first in the world in mathematics, first in science, and first in reading on the Programme for International Student Assessment — the PISA examination administered every three years by the OECD to measure the performance of education systems across seventy-two countries and economies. By 2022, with the pandemic disruption absorbed and test scores declining almost everywhere, Singapore had widened its lead. Its students scored the equivalent of three to five years of schooling ahead of the OECD average in every subject. The country that LKY had described, at independence, as "a heart with no body" — a port city severed from its hinterland, without natural resources, without a domestic market, without even reliable access to fresh water — was now producing the best-educated young people on the planet.

The educational performance of a country is not a function of its wealth. The correlation between per-pupil spending and PISA scores is weak; some of the world's richest countries post mediocre results, and some of the fastest-improving education systems are among the poorest. The performance is also not a function of class size, curriculum design, technology deployment, or any of the other variables that dominate Western education debates. The single variable that explains more variance in student outcomes than any other, across every country and every study that has attempted to isolate it, is teacher quality. A student assigned to a teacher in the top quartile of effectiveness learns roughly three times as much in a year as a student assigned to a teacher in the bottom quartile. The effect is cumulative, and it is large enough to close the achievement gap between children from wealthy and poor families within a few years of consistent exposure to effective teaching. The implication is uncomfortable for systems that have invested decades and billions of dollars in curriculum reform, technology integration, smaller class sizes, and the hundred other interventions that avoid the central question: who is standing in front of the classroom, and how were they selected, trained, and evaluated. But the evidence is not subtle. Teacher quality explains more of the variance in educational outcomes than all other in-school factors combined, and the systems that produce the best results are the systems that are most deliberate about recruiting, developing, and retaining the best teachers. Singapore is the clearest example in the world.

LKY's approach to education was not informed by educational research. It was informed by the brute fact that Singapore had no other resource. "A nation's wealth," he said shortly after independence, "is not in its land or its minerals, but in its people." The statement was not a platitude. It was a strategic assessment made by a leader whose country possessed none of the other inputs that nations rely on for survival and growth. Every other resource — capital, technology, markets, even water — could be imported. Human capability could not. The quality of Singapore's people would determine whether the country survived or disappeared, and the quality of its people was a function of the quality of its education system, which was a function of the quality of its teachers. The chain of causation was short, direct, and, in LKY's analysis, inescapable. A country that failed to recruit its best minds into teaching was a country that was systematically degrading its only resource. The logic was not ideological. It was the logic of a man who had concluded, after watching the British surrender Singapore to the Japanese in 1942 without a fight that could have made a difference, that a country's survival depended on the competence of its people and its leaders, and that competence was not a gift. It was built, deliberately, through institutions that identified talent, developed it, and deployed it where it could do the most good.

The mechanism Singapore built to implement this logic was straightforward, and it has remained essentially unchanged for more than four decades. Only the top third of each graduating cohort is admitted to teacher training. The National Institute of Education, the country's sole teacher training institution, is selective to a degree that would be politically impossible in most Western democracies, where teacher preparation programs are often the least selective professional schools in the university system and where efforts to raise admission standards are routinely defeated by the argument that selectivity would reduce the supply of teachers. Singapore's response to this objection was that the argument rested on a confusion: the relevant variable is not the number of teachers but the quality of teaching, and a system that prioritizes filling classrooms over the quality of the people filling them is a system that has accepted mediocrity as its operating standard. The country trains exactly the number of teachers it needs, selects them from the applicants with the strongest academic records and demonstrated aptitude for teaching, and pays them salaries competitive with the private-sector professions — engineering, finance, law — that would otherwise recruit the same talent. The starting salary for a teacher in Singapore is benchmarked to the starting salary for a graduate in the civil service and the corporate sector, and it rises along a career ladder that rewards demonstrated effectiveness rather than seniority. The result is that teaching in Singapore is not a fallback profession. It is a competitive career, chosen by some of the country's most capable graduates, and the status it carries — in a society famously attentive to status — reflects the importance the state assigns to it.

The outcomes this system produces are not limited to PISA scores, though PISA scores are the most visible and the most easily compared. Singapore's education system also produces one of the lowest rates of youth unemployment in the developed world, one of the highest rates of postsecondary participation, and a workforce whose skill profile has been able to attract the foreign investment — in semiconductor fabrication, in biomedical research, in financial services, in logistics — that a resource-poor country needs to maintain its position in the global economy. The system is not perfect. LKY himself acknowledged, late in his life, that the intense pressure the system placed on students — the examinations, the streaming, the competition that began in primary school and never relented — was a genuine cost, and that the country needed to find ways to reduce it without reducing the standards the pressure was designed to maintain. The tension between excellence and well-being is not unique to Singapore, but Singapore's version of it is more intense than most, because the stakes are higher. A country with oil can afford an education system that produces mediocre results for a generation. A country whose only resource is its people cannot.

LKY's critique of Western education followed from the same logic. The problem, as he saw it, was not that Western countries spent too little on education or that their teachers were underqualified — though in many cases both were true. The problem was that Western education systems had adopted a philosophy that made teacher quality almost impossible to achieve, because the philosophy denied that teacher quality was the relevant variable. Progressive education — the theory that children learn best when they direct their own learning, that the teacher's role is to facilitate rather than instruct, that standardized testing suppresses creativity, that ranking students damages self-esteem, that the purpose of education is personal fulfillment rather than national capacity — had, by the 1990s, captured the educational establishments of most English-speaking countries and was spreading through continental Europe. LKY's objection was not that progressive education was philosophically wrong, though he believed it was. His objection was that it produced worse outcomes by every available measure, and that the advocates of progressive education were indifferent to the evidence. A teacher who believes that instruction is secondary to facilitation will teach less, and students will learn less. A system that refuses to measure student performance, or that measures it but refuses to publish the results, or that publishes the results but refuses to connect them to teacher evaluation, has disabled the feedback loops that make improvement possible. A system that treats self-esteem as an input rather than an output — that tries to make students feel successful rather than teaching them to be successful — is producing the psychological equivalent of a currency that is not backed by reserves. The feeling may circulate for a while, but it cannot be exchanged for anything real.

The results of this philosophy, in LKY's assessment, were visible in the international comparisons that began to accumulate from the 1990s onward. The United States, which spent more per pupil than almost any country in the world, consistently posted PISA scores in the middle of the developed-country distribution, with the performance of its lowest-income students comparable to that of developing countries. The United Kingdom, which had pioneered the progressive reforms LKY found most damaging, saw its relative position decline steadily over the decades in which those reforms were implemented. France, Germany, and the Scandinavian countries — each with its own variant of the progressive consensus — posted results that were respectable but unexceptional, and in several cases declining. Meanwhile, the countries that were systematically recruiting their best graduates into teaching, training them rigorously, and holding them accountable for student outcomes — Singapore, South Korea, Japan, and, increasingly, China — were clustered at the top of every international ranking, year after year, in a pattern that was too consistent to be accidental and too persistent to be explained by cultural differences alone. The pattern was structural, and the structure was the teaching profession: who entered it, how they were trained, and whether they were treated as professionals whose performance mattered or as civil servants whose presence was sufficient.

The education system is one of the load-bearing walls of a society, and like any load-bearing wall, its failure is not immediately visible. A generation of mediocre education does not produce an immediate crisis. It produces a generation of adults who are slightly less capable than the generation they replaced, slightly less able to compete in the global economy, slightly less equipped to solve the problems the society faces. The effects are incremental, and because they are incremental, they are easy to ignore, to explain away, to attribute to factors beyond the control of policymakers — technology, globalization, demographic change, the thousand external forces that provide convenient cover for institutional failure. But the effects are also cumulative, and after three or four generations of incremental decline, a society discovers that it no longer possesses the human capability to do what it once could do. The infrastructure that supported its prosperity — its engineering workforce, its scientific establishment, its managerial competence, its institutional memory — has been gradually depleted, and the depletion is only visible when it is too late to reverse it. A country with natural resources can survive this depletion for a long time, because natural resources can be extracted with relatively low levels of human capability. A country whose only resource is its people cannot survive it at all. Singapore understood this from the day of its founding, because it had no choice. The West, which has always had choices, has spent the past half-century pretending that the quality of its education system is a discretionary matter rather than an existential one. The PISA results suggest that the pretense is becoming unsustainable.
