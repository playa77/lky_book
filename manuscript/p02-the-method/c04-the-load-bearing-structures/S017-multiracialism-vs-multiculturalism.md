The distinction LKY drew between multiracialism and multiculturalism was not semantic. It was the difference between a society that can hold together and one that cannot, and he drew it with the clarity of someone who had governed a country that nearly tore itself apart along racial lines in the year before independence. The 1964 race riots had killed thirty-six people. The memory was not abstract. The question was not theoretical. A country in which Chinese, Malays, and Indians lived in separate neighborhoods, spoke different languages, attended different schools, and held different political loyalties was a country that would be destroyed by its diversity rather than strengthened by it. The only alternative was to build a shared identity deliberately — to engineer integration, enforce it where necessary, and create institutions that made cooperation across racial lines a daily fact of life rather than an aspiration.

This is what multiracialism meant in Singapore. It did not mean celebrating diversity in the sense of encouraging each community to maintain its distinct identity and parallel institutions. It meant building a single civic identity — Singaporean — that was more salient than Chinese, Malay, or Indian identity in the domains that mattered for collective survival: housing, education, national service, political participation. The state did not merely encourage this. It mandated it, and it did so through mechanisms that were deliberately, visibly, and sometimes coercively engineered. HDB ethnic quotas ensured that every apartment block and every neighborhood reflected the national racial composition — approximately seventy-four percent Chinese, thirteen percent Malay, nine percent Indian, with the remainder classified as Others. A unit could not be sold to a buyer whose ethnic group had already reached its quota in that block. The policy was a direct restriction on property rights, justified on the grounds that unrestricted property rights would reproduce the segregated neighborhoods that had produced the 1964 riots. English was established as the common working language of government, law, and commerce — not because English was culturally neutral, but because it was equally foreign to all three of Singapore's major communities, and therefore equally fair. Mandarin, Malay, and Tamil were preserved as required second languages in the education system, but the language of public life was one that belonged to no single racial group, and the decision was enforced through a school system that made English the primary medium of instruction. And national service — two years of mandatory military duty for every male citizen — placed young men of every race in the same barracks, under the same discipline, with the same obligations, eating the same food and performing the same tasks. LKY considered this the single most effective instrument of nation-building ever devised, and he was probably right.

The results were not utopian. Racial prejudice persisted, as it persists everywhere. Informal social segregation — in marriage patterns, in friendship networks, in the private preferences that no quota system can reach — remained significant. But the outcome that mattered — the absence of ethnic violence, the functioning of a shared civic space, the ability of citizens of different races to cooperate in institutions that affected their lives — was achieved. Singapore has not experienced a race riot since 1969, a year in which spillover violence from Malaysian elections briefly flared and was suppressed. In a region where ethnic conflict has produced some of the worst atrocities of the postwar period — Indonesia in 1965 and 1998, Myanmar in 2017, Malaysia in 1969 — Singapore's record of ethnic peace is not a coincidence. It is the product of deliberate institutional design, maintained at the cost of illiberal policies that constrained individual choice in the service of collective survival. Whether the tradeoff was justified is a question every society must answer for itself, using its own circumstances and its own standards. That the tradeoff existed, that Singapore made it explicitly, and that it worked by the only standard that ultimately counts — the standard of outcomes — is not seriously disputable. A country founded in the aftermath of race riots, surrounded by larger neighbors for whom ethnic identity was the organizing principle of politics, has not experienced ethnic violence in more than fifty years. The mechanisms that produced that outcome were illiberal. So was the alternative.

Multiculturalism, in LKY's analysis, was the opposite approach — and the one that Western democracies had adopted, largely by default rather than design, over the second half of the twentieth century. The model was not engineered integration but parallel coexistence. Immigrant communities were permitted, and in some formulations encouraged, to maintain their distinct languages, cultural practices, and institutional arrangements, with the expectation that a thin layer of shared civic values — democracy, tolerance, rule of law — would be sufficient to hold the society together. The expectation was wrong, and the evidence for its wrongness accumulated steadily across Western Europe over the subsequent decades.

Britain provided the clearest early example, and the one LKY cited most frequently. Postwar immigration from the Caribbean and South Asia was managed, in practice, as labor importation without integration. Immigrants were housed in declining inner-city neighborhoods that the white working class was vacating, forming concentrated communities that, over two or three generations, became structurally separate from the majority society. Separate schools — de facto rather than de jure — became the norm in many cities, with some schools in Bradford and Leicester enrolling student bodies that were over ninety percent from a single ethnic background. The 2001 race riots in Bradford, Burnley, and Oldham were not random outbreaks of violence but the product of communities that had been living, in the official phrase of the subsequent government inquiry, "parallel lives." The Cantle Report found that residents of these towns led "a series of parallel lives" in which they "do not interact, have little knowledge of each other, and inhabit separate social, cultural, and economic worlds." The report's central recommendation — that integration required deliberate intervention through shared institutions, common spaces, and an explicit civic identity — was essentially the Singaporean position, delivered thirty-seven years after the 1964 riots and without the political will or institutional capacity to implement it.

France's experience was different in form but convergent in outcome. The republican model of integration — universal citizenship, secular public institutions, the deliberate refusal to recognize ethnic or religious identity in official life — was, in theory, the opposite of Anglo-American multiculturalism. In practice, it produced the same result: concentrated immigrant communities in the banlieues on the urban periphery, spatially and economically excluded from the opportunities the republican model promised but could not deliver. Color-blind universalism, it turned out, was blind to discrimination as well as to identity, and the refusal to collect official statistics on race or ethnicity made it impossible to measure the problem, let alone design interventions to address it. The 2005 riots, which spread across three hundred French towns and cities over three weeks and resulted in the burning of thousands of vehicles and the declaration of a state of emergency, were a declaration of failure from a model that had insisted it could not fail because it did not recognize the categories in which failure was occurring. The German experience with Turkish guest workers — imported as temporary labor under agreements signed in the 1960s, never provided with a path to citizenship, never integrated into German civic life, left to form permanent parallel communities without political representation or cultural membership — produced a different configuration of the same structural problem: diversity without integration capacity, sustained for decades, producing mutual alienation that hardened into mutual antagonism. Sweden, widely regarded as the most generous and progressive of European immigration states, took in more asylum seekers per capita than any other European country during the 2015 migration crisis and discovered, by the end of the decade, that generosity without integration infrastructure produced segregated neighborhoods with unemployment rates exceeding fifty percent among some immigrant populations, gang violence that the Swedish welfare state had no institutional capacity to address, and the rise of the Sweden Democrats, a party with neo-Nazi origins that became the country's third-largest political force.

The pattern across these cases was consistent, and it was the pattern LKY had predicted decades before the evidence accumulated to the point of undeniability. Immigration without integration infrastructure is not immigration. It is the importation of diversity without the institutional capacity to make diversity functional. A society that admits large numbers of people from different cultural backgrounds and makes no serious effort to integrate them — to equip them with the language, the economic participation, the civic identity, and the shared experience that make cooperation across difference possible — is running an experiment whose results have been available for at least two generations. The results are not ambiguous. Parallel societies produce mutual suspicion. Mutual suspicion becomes political polarization. Political polarization makes collective action on any problem requiring cross-community sacrifice effectively impossible, and the problems that most urgently require cross-community sacrifice — climate adaptation, fiscal sustainability, the integration of the next wave of migrants — become harder to address the longer the polarization persists. The cycle is not self-correcting. It is self-reinforcing, and the endpoint is a society that can no longer act collectively on any question that divides it, which means it can no longer act collectively on any question that matters.

The Singaporean alternative was not cruelty. It was clarity. The state told immigrants what was expected of them, provided the institutions that made meeting those expectations possible, and enforced the expectations when they were not met. Permanent residency and citizenship were not entitlements. They were earned through demonstrated contribution, linguistic competence, and cultural integration, and they could be revoked. The country's immigration policy was calibrated to economic need — admitting workers when the economy required them, restricting admission when it didn't, and never pretending that immigration was a humanitarian program rather than an economic and strategic one. The approach was unsentimental, and it was functional, and the societies that adopted the opposite approach — sentiment without structure, generosity without integration capacity — are now living with the consequences.

LKY's view on this question was not popular in Western circles during his lifetime, and it is not popular now. But it has the uncomfortable property of having been correct about outcomes that the Western consensus was wrong about, and the gap between the consensus and the outcomes is widening. The question that his distinction between multiracialism and multiculturalism poses is not whether Western societies should adopt HDB ethnic quotas or mandatory national service. It is whether they are capable of being honest about what integration requires, and whether they have the institutional capacity and the political will to provide it. The evidence to date suggests the answer, in most cases, is no — and that societies that cannot answer yes are societies that will be governed by the consequences of their diversity rather than the benefits of it.
