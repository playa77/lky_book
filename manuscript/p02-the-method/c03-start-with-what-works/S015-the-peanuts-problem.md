LKY had a phrase for the relationship between compensation and competence in government. It was characteristically direct, characteristically unsentimental, and characteristically correct. "You pay peanuts," he said, "you get monkeys." The aphorism has been repeated so often that it has lost some of its force, which is unfortunate, because it is not a joke. It is a structural observation about the single highest-leverage decision any political system makes: whom it recruits to govern, how it selects them, and what it pays them to stay.

Singapore's approach to this question was unusual in its honesty. Most political systems pretend that talented people should want to govern out of a sense of duty, and that the compensation is secondary. LKY considered this naive — and, more damagingly, dishonest. The people a country needs to govern it are the same people the private sector is trying to hire, and the private sector offers compensation, autonomy, and prestige that most governments cannot match. If government salaries are set at levels that only the independently wealthy or the incompetent can afford to accept, the result will be a governing class composed of the independently wealthy and the incompetent, in proportions that vary by country but that converge on the same outcome: a government that cannot do its job. Singapore's solution was to benchmark ministerial and senior civil service salaries to the private sector — specifically, to the top earners in professions from which the government needed to recruit. The salaries were high. They were also public, deliberately so. LKY wanted citizens to know what their government cost, and he wanted them to compare it to the cost of the alternative. The alternative, he argued, was a government staffed by people the private sector didn't want — and in a country with no resources except its people, that was not a risk. It was a suicide pact.

The compensation was not a gift. It came with expectations that would be considered oppressive in most Western democracies. Ministers and senior civil servants were expected to be competent, honest, and available essentially without limit. Performance failures resulted in removal. Corruption — defined broadly, including conduct that "brought the government into disrepute" — resulted in prosecution and the destruction of the individual's career, reputation, and finances. The deal was explicit: the state would make you rich, and in exchange, the state would own you. You would work at the pleasure of the prime minister, you would be held to standards of conduct that extended into your private life, and if you failed — in performance or in probity — the consequences would be public, permanent, and severe. The number of people willing to accept this deal was small, which was the point. The government was not trying to attract everyone. It was trying to attract the small number of people who were good enough to govern and disciplined enough to accept the constraints that genuine accountability imposed.

The results were measurable. The Corrupt Practices Investigation Bureau, established under the British and given extraordinary powers by LKY's government, pursued corruption with an intensity that was almost unique in the postcolonial world. Ministers were investigated. Senior civil servants were prosecuted. The prime minister himself was subject to inquiry. The message was unambiguous: the salary bought performance and probity, and the absence of either voided the transaction. Singapore went from a country where bribery was a routine cost of doing business to a country ranked consistently among the least corrupt on earth in a single generation. This was not a cultural transformation. It was an institutional one, and the institution that made it possible was the one that paid enough to attract talent and punished enough to deter abuse.

The Western contrast is not flattering. In most Western democracies, political salaries are low relative to the private-sector opportunities available to the people the government needs to recruit. The result is a political class that is disproportionately composed of the independently wealthy, the professionally mediocre, and the ideologically committed — none of which is a reliable predictor of governing competence. The independently wealthy may be talented, but they are insulated from the consequences of their decisions in ways that make accountability difficult. The professionally mediocre are, by definition, not the best available people. The ideologically committed are motivated by beliefs rather than outcomes, which is the definition of the pathology LKY spent his career diagnosing. A system that selects from these three pools will, over time, produce a governing class that is less competent than the problems it faces, and it will continue to do so because the selection mechanism that produces it is self-reinforcing: competent people observe the compensation and the constraints and rationally choose not to participate, leaving the field to those for whom the compensation is sufficient or the constraints are not binding.

The cost of this arrangement is not measured in the salaries saved. It is measured in the outcomes foregone. A government staffed by the second-tier makes second-tier decisions, and second-tier decisions compound. A mediocre infrastructure decision costs billions over decades. A mediocre education decision costs a generation of human capital. A mediocre regulatory decision costs an industry's competitiveness. LKY's argument was that the salary savings from underpaying politicians were the most expensive savings a country could make, because they purchased incompetence at a discount that was not a discount when the bill arrived. The bill always arrived.

This chapter has traced the operating system — the method by which LKY approached governance as an engineering problem, assembled solutions from any available source, and designed institutions that combined mechanisms Western politics treats as incompatible. The operating system worked. But it worked because it was implemented by people who were competent enough to implement it, and the precondition for that competence was a talent system that attracted, developed, and retained the best available people. The next chapter examines what those people built — the load-bearing structures of family, culture, and education that LKY considered the foundations on which everything else depended.
