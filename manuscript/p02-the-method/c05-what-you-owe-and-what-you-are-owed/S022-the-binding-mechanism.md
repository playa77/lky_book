Of all the institutions LKY built, the one he considered most essential was not the Central Provident Fund, not the Housing Development Board, not the education system, and not the Corrupt Practices Investigation Bureau, although he considered each of them indispensable. The institution he considered most essential was national service. The reason was not primarily military, though the military function was real and the threat environment that Singapore faced — a city-state of two hundred and twenty square miles, surrounded by much larger neighbors with whom relations had been, at various points, openly hostile — meant that a credible defense force was not optional. But the deeper reason, and the one LKY returned to whenever he was asked to identify the single policy that had done the most to make Singapore a nation rather than a collection of ethnic enclaves, was that national service was the only institution in Singaporean life that placed the children of every race and every class in the same position, under the same authority, with the same obligations, for a period long enough to make the experience formative. A Chinese university graduate and a Malay school-leaver and an Indian polytechnic student, who would never have met in any other circumstance, spent two years eating the same food, performing the same drills, enduring the same hardships, and learning — in a way that no classroom could teach — that their fates were bound together. The binding was not metaphorical. It was literal, enforced by the state, and LKY considered it the closest thing to a guarantee of national cohesion that a multiracial society could produce.

The argument against national service is well known, and it is not trivial. Compelling young people to interrupt their education or the start of their careers for two years of military service is a restriction on individual liberty of the most direct kind. It imposes an economic cost on the individuals who serve, who enter the workforce later and with lower lifetime earnings than they would have if they had not served. It imposes an economic cost on the society, which loses two years of productive labor from every cohort of young men. It is open to the objection that a professional military — a smaller, better-trained, better-equipped force of volunteers — is more militarily effective than a conscript force, and the objection has been borne out by the experience of every modern war in which professional armies have fought conscript armies. And it is open to the objection that compulsory service is fundamentally illiberal, incompatible with the principle that the state exists to protect individual freedom rather than to commandeer it, and that a society that takes two years of a young person's life without his consent is a society that has crossed a line that democratic societies should not cross. These arguments are not straw men. They are the arguments that were used to end conscription in the United States in 1973, in the United Kingdom in 1960, in France in 2001, and in Germany in 2011, and they were advanced in each case by serious people who believed that the abolition of compulsory military service was a victory for liberty, for economic efficiency, and for military effectiveness. They were, in each of these dimensions, at least partly right.

What the abolitionists did not anticipate, and what LKY did, was that national service was doing something that no other institution in modern society does. It was forcing people who would otherwise never interact — the rich and the poor, the educated and the uneducated, the urban and the rural, the native-born and the immigrant-descended, the members of every ethnic and religious group in a diverse society — to live together, work together, and depend on each other under conditions of shared difficulty and shared purpose. The barracks and the training ground were not merely sites of military instruction. They were the only institutions in modern life where the class segregation that defined every other domain — where the wealthy lived, where their children went to school, who they married, who they worked with, who they socialized with — was systematically broken. An eighteen-year-old from a wealthy suburb of Chicago and an eighteen-year-old from a poor neighborhood in the South Bronx would never meet in civilian life. They would attend different schools, live in different neighborhoods, consume different media, develop different frames of reference, and form different understandings of what their country was and what it owed them. Under conscription, they would meet in basic training. They would share a barracks. They would learn, in ways that no amount of civics education could replicate, that the other was not an abstraction — a statistic in a newspaper article about inequality, a figure in a political debate about welfare — but a human being with whom they shared a common task and a common fate. The experience did not eliminate class differences or racial tensions. But it made them less abstract, which made them less dangerous, because the abstract hatred of people you have never met is a more powerful political force than the complicated feelings you have about people you have lived with.

The abolition of conscription in the Western democracies produced, over the subsequent decades, an effect that was gradual, cumulative, and almost invisible in any given year but unmistakable when viewed across the half-century that has elapsed since most of the decisions were made. The effect was the elimination of the only institution in modern Western life that forced sustained cross-class and cross-racial contact among young adults. The military became a professional institution, staffed disproportionately by recruits from lower-income and rural backgrounds, with officer corps drawn increasingly from military families and military academies — a closed institutional culture with its own norms, its own values, and its own relationship to the civilian society that it served but no longer represented. The civilian society, meanwhile, became more segregated by class, by education, by geography, and by political affiliation, and the segregation was self-reinforcing. People who never met anyone different from themselves developed political preferences that reflected their isolation, and the political preferences produced policies that deepened the isolation. The result, after two generations, was a society in which the elites who made decisions about military deployments had never served in the military, and the people who served in the military had never been part of the elite, and the two groups had no shared experience, no common frame of reference, and no basis for understanding each other's interests or perspectives. The civilian-military divide that has become a subject of anxious commentary in Washington, London, and Paris is not a problem that appeared suddenly. It is the predictable consequence of a half-century in which the only institution that forced the two groups into contact was dismantled, and nothing was built to replace it.

Singapore did not make the same choice, because Singapore could not afford to. A city-state with no strategic depth, no natural barriers, and neighbors whose intentions could not be guaranteed required a military large enough to make invasion costly, and a military that large required conscription. But the military requirement was not the only one, and LKY was explicit about the fact that if the military requirement had been the only one, he would have designed the institution differently. National service was also, and in his view equally, a nation-building instrument, and its nation-building function was not a byproduct of its military function. It was a co-equal purpose, deliberately designed into the institution from the beginning. The two functions were inseparable, and the separation of the two — the creation of a professional military that performed the defense function while the society performed whatever social integration it could manage through voluntary means — was, in LKY's analysis, a mistake whose costs were borne over decades and whose full consequences had not yet been felt. A society that outsources its defense to professionals is a society that has outsourced the experience of shared sacrifice, and a society that has outsourced the experience of shared sacrifice is a society that will eventually discover that it has no shared experience at all. The discovery, when it comes, will arrive at a moment of crisis — a war, a depression, a pandemic, a natural disaster — when shared sacrifice is suddenly required, and the institutional machinery for producing it no longer exists.
