The obligations framework LKY built was not designed for easy times. It was designed for the conditions Singapore faced at its founding — precarious, resource-poor, surrounded by larger and potentially hostile neighbors, with no margin for error and no external guarantor of its survival. The framework's demandingness was not a feature that could be softened when conditions improved. It was the feature that made survival possible under conditions that did not improve for a generation, and it was maintained after conditions did improve because LKY understood something that comfortable societies forget: the stress test that determines whether a society's institutions are adequate is not the stress test they were designed to pass. It is the stress test they will face next, and the next stress test is always harder than the last one, because the problems that accumulate during periods of stability are always more complex than the problems that were solved during the period of crisis that produced the stability.

The twenty-first century is administering a stress test that the citizen-as-consumer model was not designed to pass, and the indicators suggest that it is failing. The test has three components, and each compounds the others. The first is demographic. The developed democracies are aging rapidly, with fertility rates below replacement in every country in Western Europe, North America, and East Asia outside of Israel. The ratio of workers to retirees is declining, which means that the tax base that funds the entitlements the citizen-as-consumer model has promised is shrinking relative to the population that receives them. The mathematics are inescapable: fewer workers supporting more retirees with more expensive entitlements, financed by debt that is already at levels that would have been considered catastrophic a generation ago, on a trajectory that points in one direction. The second component is geopolitical. The great-power competition that the end of the Cold War was supposed to have rendered obsolete has returned, and it has returned in a form that is more expensive and more demanding than the version that preceded it. The military capabilities required to deter a peer competitor in the Pacific, to defend allies in Europe, to maintain the technological edge that has underwritten Western security for seventy years, and to secure the supply chains on which the Western economies depend are capabilities that require sustained investment over decades, and the investment competes directly with the entitlements that the citizen-as-consumer model has made politically untouchable. The third component is technological, and it is the variable that transforms the first two from serious problems into existential ones.

Artificial general intelligence — the development of machine intelligence that matches or exceeds human capability across the full range of cognitive tasks — is the third component, and it is the one LKY did not live to see. He died in 2015, before the deep learning revolution had produced the language models, the reasoning systems, and the autonomous agents that have, in the decade since his death, transformed the question of AGI from a speculative concern of the distant future into a near-term strategic reality. The technology is not hypothetical. It is under active development by multiple state and corporate actors, with timelines that have shortened dramatically over the past five years and capabilities that have exceeded the predictions of every forecast published before 2020. The implications of this technology for governance are not incremental. They are structural. An economy in which a significant fraction of cognitive labor can be performed by machines is an economy in which the relationship between work and income — the relationship that has organized every society since the Industrial Revolution — no longer holds in its current form. The political and social consequences of that transformation, in a society whose political stability depends on the citizen-as-consumer bargain, are not difficult to foresee. A population that has been promised entitlements it cannot afford, that has been trained to treat its relationship to the state as transactional, that has lost the habits of shared sacrifice and reciprocal obligation, and that is suddenly confronted with an economic transformation that renders a large fraction of its members economically superfluous, is a population that will not respond to the transformation with stoic acceptance. It will respond with demands that cannot be met, directed at institutions that cannot meet them, in a political system whose legitimacy depends on meeting them. The formula is not stable, and the instability is not a distant prospect.

The alternative that LKY's framework offers is not a prediction. It is a design principle. A society that has maintained the institutions of reciprocal obligation — that has preserved the expectation that citizens owe service, lawfulness, and contribution in exchange for security, opportunity, and infrastructure — is a society that possesses the institutional machinery for asking citizens to accept sacrifice when circumstances require it. The machinery does not guarantee that the sacrifice will be accepted. But it makes the request possible, and the possibility is the difference between a society that can adapt to the conditions the twenty-first century is imposing and a society that will be broken by them. The question that the remainder of this book addresses is whether the adaptation can be made, and what the consequences will be if it cannot.
