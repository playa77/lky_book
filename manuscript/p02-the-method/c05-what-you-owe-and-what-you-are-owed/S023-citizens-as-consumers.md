The citizen-as-consumer is not a metaphor. It is a description of a political relationship that has, over the past half-century, replaced the older relationship between citizen and state in most Western democracies, and it describes the replacement with precision. In the older relationship, which survived in recognizable form through the middle decades of the twentieth century, the citizen owed the state certain obligations — taxes, military service when required, obedience to the law, participation in the institutions of civic life — and the state owed the citizen certain goods — security, infrastructure, a legal framework within which to pursue private ends, a safety net for those who could not provide for themselves. The relationship was reciprocal, and the reciprocity was the mechanism that made it stable. Each side had something to lose if the relationship broke down, and each side had reason to maintain it. In the citizen-as-consumer relationship, the reciprocity has been severed on one side and attenuated on the other. The citizen continues to receive the goods — now vastly expanded, now including not merely the safety net of the midcentury welfare state but a comprehensive package of entitlements that covers health care, retirement income, education, housing subsidies, and a growing catalog of programs that address needs previously considered private — but the obligations that balanced the receipt of those goods have been steadily reduced. The citizen-as-consumer pays taxes, but the taxes are insufficient to cover the cost of the goods received, and the shortfall is covered by debt. The citizen-as-consumer is subject to the law, but the law is increasingly treated as a constraint to be optimized around rather than an obligation to be honored. The citizen-as-consumer is asked to serve, but service is voluntary, and the voluntary nature of the request transforms it from an obligation into a preference, which most citizens exercising their preferences decline. The result is a relationship in which one party receives, the other party provides, and the providing party finances the provision by borrowing against the future earnings of the receiving party's children. The word for this is not a social contract. It is a transfer, and the transfer runs in one direction.

The mechanism that makes the transfer possible is public debt. The governments of the developed democracies have, over the past several decades, financed an expanding set of entitlements not by raising the taxes required to pay for them but by borrowing the difference, and the borrowing has accumulated to levels that would have been considered catastrophic a generation ago and are now considered normal. The United States federal debt exceeds one hundred and twenty percent of gross domestic product. Japan's exceeds two hundred and fifty percent. Italy, Greece, and several other European states are in comparable territory, and the trajectory is uniformly upward. The debt is not an accident of circumstance. It is a structural feature of the citizen-as-consumer model, because the model requires that the goods provided to current citizens exceed the revenues those citizens are willing to contribute, and the only way to close the gap is to borrow. The borrowing is, in effect, taxation without representation: the people who will be required to service the debt — through higher taxes, reduced services, or inflation — are not the people who authorized it. They are the citizens' children and grandchildren, who were not present when the borrowing decisions were made, who cannot vote against them, and who will inherit the obligations without having received the benefits. The citizen-as-consumer model is, in its fiscal dimension, an intergenerational transfer from the young to the old, from the future to the present, from people who have no political power to people who have all of it. It is the opposite of the reciprocal relationship that defined the older social contract, and it has the advantage, for the people who benefit from it, of being politically irresistible. A politician who proposes to reduce entitlements in order to reduce the debt that will fall on future generations is asking current voters to accept less so that people who cannot yet vote will have more. The incentives point in one direction, and they have been pointing in that direction for long enough that the cumulative effect is no longer marginal.

The fiscal dimension of the citizen-as-consumer model is reinforced by a cultural dimension that is harder to measure but at least as consequential. Tax compliance in most Western democracies remains high by global standards, but tax avoidance — the lawful minimization of tax liability through mechanisms that are available disproportionately to the wealthy and the well-advised — has become a cultural norm in the professional classes, and tax evasion — the unlawful concealment of income or assets — is widespread enough that the estimated gap between taxes owed and taxes collected runs to hundreds of billions of dollars annually in the United States alone. The cultural posture that underlies these behaviors is not primarily economic. It is not that the people who avoid or evade taxes cannot afford to pay them. It is that they regard taxation as a taking rather than an obligation, a cost to be minimized rather than a contribution to be made, and the attitude is reinforced by a political discourse in which taxation is routinely described as theft, as confiscation, as the coercive extraction of resources from productive citizens by an unproductive state. The discourse is not new. What is new is that it has migrated from the political fringe to the political center, and that it has been adopted, with varying degrees of explicitness, by the professional classes that benefit most from the infrastructure, the legal system, the educated workforce, and the stable currency that the state provides. The citizen who demands the services of the state while treating the state's claim on his resources as illegitimate is not a citizen. He is a free rider, and a society in which free riding becomes a cultural norm is a society that has lost the concept of reciprocity on which its institutions depend.

The concept of reciprocity is the core of the older social contract that the citizen-as-consumer model has displaced, and its erosion is the deepest pathology of the new arrangement. In a reciprocal relationship, each party's obligations are the justification for the other party's benefits. The citizen's obligation to pay taxes is the justification for the state's provision of infrastructure and security. The citizen's obligation to obey the law is the justification for the state's protection of the citizen's rights. The citizen's obligation to serve — in the military, in civic institutions, in the thousand small acts of participation that make collective life possible — is the justification for the state's claim to represent the citizen's interests. When the obligations are weakened, the justifications weaken with them, and the result is a relationship that is experienced, on both sides, as exploitation. The citizen experiences the state as an extractive entity that takes without giving enough in return, because the citizen has lost the sense that his contributions are part of a system of mutual obligation. The state experiences the citizen as a demanding consumer whose expectations are unlimited and whose willingness to contribute is minimal, because the state has lost the legitimacy that comes from enforcing obligations rather than merely servicing preferences. The relationship becomes transactional in the narrowest sense — what am I getting, what am I paying — and because the transaction is structured so that the citizen gets more than he pays for, the transaction is unsustainable. The unsustainability is visible in the debt. It is visible in the infrastructure that is not maintained, the public services that are not funded, the crises that are not prepared for because preparation requires current sacrifice for future benefit and the citizen-as-consumer model has no mechanism for requiring current sacrifice. And it is becoming visible, with increasing clarity, in the political turmoil that has characterized the Western democracies over the past decade — the rise of populist movements that promise to restore what has been lost, the erosion of trust in institutions that are seen as vehicles for extraction rather than instruments of mutual obligation, the growing sense among citizens that the deal has been broken and that no one knows how to fix it.

LKY's version of the deal — explicit, enforced, non-negotiable — was designed to prevent precisely this sequence of developments. A society that states its obligations clearly, enforces them impartially, and refuses to allow them to become optional is a society that maintains the reciprocity on which collective survival depends. A society that allows obligations to become preferences, that finances current consumption with future debt, and that treats the citizen's relationship to the state as a transaction in which the citizen's side of the ledger is always net positive is a society that is borrowing not merely money but legitimacy, and the debt in both currencies comes due at the same time. The question that the citizen-as-consumer model poses is not whether it is fair. It is whether it can survive the stress tests that the twenty-first century is administering, and the evidence, as the stresses intensify, is that it cannot.
