Patriotism, in LKY's vocabulary, was not a sentiment. It was a capability. A patriotic population was not one that felt warmly toward its country, displayed flags on national holidays, or experienced a swelling of emotion during the national anthem. It was a population that was willing, when circumstances required it, to accept sacrifice for the collective's survival — to pay higher taxes, to serve in the military, to defer consumption, to subordinate private interest to public necessity. The feeling was irrelevant except insofar as it produced the behavior, and the behavior was what the country needed. A population that loved its country but refused to sacrifice for it was patriotic in the only sense that did not matter. A population that was willing to sacrifice — regardless of how it felt — was patriotic in the only sense that did.

Singapore did not have the raw materials from which patriotism is usually made. It had no ancient history, no shared ethnicity, no common language, no founding myth that predated living memory, no territory that extended beyond the horizon of a single city. It had been expelled from Malaysia in 1965 against the wishes of its own leadership, and the expulsion had been experienced, by the population that suddenly found itself citizens of an unwanted country, as an abandonment rather than a liberation. LKY wept on live television when he announced the separation, and the tears were not for the cameras. They were the tears of a man who believed he had just been handed the responsibility for a country that could not survive, and the population that watched him weep shared the assessment. A population that does not believe its country can survive does not sacrifice for it voluntarily. The patriotism Singapore required had to be manufactured, because the conditions that produce patriotism naturally — shared history, shared identity, shared threat, shared sacrifice — did not exist in sufficient quantity, and the ones that did exist had to be amplified and institutionalized until they could perform the function that centuries of organic nation-building performed in older countries.

The manufacturing was done through policy, and the policies were designed to solve two problems simultaneously. National service solved the manpower requirement of a credible defense force while also creating the shared experience that made the defended country feel like a country worth defending. The Housing Development Board solved the housing shortage while also giving every citizen a material stake in the country's survival — an asset whose value depended on the country's continued existence, a reason to care about outcomes that a renter in a country that might not exist in a decade had no reason to care about. The education system produced a skilled workforce while also teaching a common curriculum that included a national narrative — the story of how Singapore had been founded, why it had nearly failed, what its citizens owed each other, and what would happen if they stopped paying. The economic policies that produced three decades of uninterrupted growth solved the problem of material deprivation while also solving the problem of legitimacy: a government that made its citizens prosperous was a government whose survival its citizens had reason to support, and the reason was not ideological. It was practical, and practical patriotism — the patriotism of people who have decided that their country is worth defending because it has made their lives better — is more durable than the patriotism of flags and anthems, because it rests on interests rather than sentiments, and interests are more reliable than sentiments under conditions of stress.

The Western democracies once possessed a version of this practical patriotism, and they lost it. The loss was not accidental. It was the product of a political dynamic in which patriotism became the exclusive property of the political right, which used it to justify nationalism, militarism, and the exclusion of immigrants, while the political left responded by abandoning the concept entirely — treating patriotism as a pathology, national identity as a form of bigotry, and the willingness to sacrifice for one's country as a primitive impulse that enlightened people had outgrown. The dynamic was self-reinforcing. The more the right used patriotism as a weapon, the more the left treated it as a toxin, and the more the left treated it as a toxin, the more the right claimed exclusive ownership of it. The result was that a concept essential to collective action — the willingness of citizens to accept costs for the benefit of the collective — became politically toxic, and the toxicity meant that no mainstream political figure could advocate for the shared sacrifice that the country's circumstances required without being accused, by one side, of insufficient patriotic fervor and, by the other, of dangerous nationalism. The paralysis was functional before it was political. A society that cannot ask its citizens to sacrifice cannot solve problems that require sacrifice, and the problems that require sacrifice are the problems that determine whether societies survive.

Singapore could not afford this paralysis, and it did not experience it. The patriotism it manufactured was practical, transactional, and deliberately stripped of the symbolic content that made the concept volatile in Western democracies. Citizens were asked to serve, to save, to obey the law, and to contribute to the collective welfare not because the nation was glorious or its history was noble but because the nation was precarious and its survival required their effort. The appeal was to interest, not to emotion, and the interest was genuine. A Singaporean who refused national service, evaded taxes, or treated public goods as disposable was not failing to love his country. He was failing to recognize that the country's survival was his survival, and that the connection between the two was not metaphorical. It was literal, and a population that understood the connection literally did not need to be taught to love a flag. It needed to be shown that the country was worth the sacrifice it demanded, and the demonstration was the country's performance — its security, its prosperity, its capacity to deliver the goods that made citizenship valuable. Patriotism without flags is not an impoverished version of the real thing. It is the real thing, and the societies that have lost the capacity to practice it are societies that have substituted symbols for substance at the moment when substance is all that matters.
