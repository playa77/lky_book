Every society rests on a deal between the individual and the collective. The deal is usually unwritten, and in most societies, it is contested. The individual insists on what the collective owes them; the collective insists on what the individual owes it; the boundary between the two sets of claims is the permanent subject of democratic politics, and its constant renegotiation is what democratic politics exists to conduct. What made Singapore unusual was not that the deal existed — every functioning society has one — but that the deal was explicit, enforced, and non-negotiable in its essentials. LKY did not pretend that the individual's relationship to the state was a matter of preference or philosophical inclination. He stated the terms, built institutions that implemented them, and accepted the political cost of doing so. The terms were: the state would provide security, law, infrastructure, and opportunity. The citizen would provide work, lawfulness, service, and loyalty. Neither party could renegotiate its obligations without the deal collapsing, and the deal collapsing meant the country collapsing, because a city-state with no resources and hostile neighbors could not afford the luxury of a population that treated its obligations as optional.

The deal was not theoretical. It was embedded in institutions that made the obligations concrete and the benefits visible. National service — two years of mandatory military duty for every male citizen, followed by annual reserve training until the age of forty — was the most demanding obligation and the one LKY considered most essential. It was not merely a military policy. It was a statement about the terms of citizenship: the country would defend you, but you would defend the country, and the obligation was not a matter of choice or class or political affiliation. The children of cabinet ministers served alongside the children of factory workers, and the shared experience of service — the same food, the same discipline, the same exposure to discomfort and danger — was the closest thing Singapore had to a common civic sacrament. The Central Provident Fund, the mandatory savings system that required every worker to set aside a substantial portion of income for retirement, healthcare, and housing, was the economic expression of the same principle. The state would not allow you to become dependent on it, and it would not allow you to become a burden on others. In exchange, it ensured that your savings were adequate to your needs, that your housing was affordable and your healthcare was funded, and that the dignity that came from self-sufficiency was available to everyone who met the terms. The Housing Development Board, which built and sold public housing to eighty percent of the population, was not a welfare program. It was a mechanism for converting the obligation to save into the asset of home ownership, and the asset of home ownership into the stake that made citizenship meaningful. A citizen who owned a home in a country that could be invaded by its neighbors had a material interest in that country's survival that a tenant did not. The deal was transactional, and the transaction was deliberate.

The Western formulation of the social contract is different, and the difference is not merely one of emphasis. The Western tradition, from Locke through Rousseau to Rawls, begins with the individual and asks what the individual would consent to give up in exchange for the benefits of collective life. The rights of the individual are the starting point; the obligations of the citizen are derived from them, and they are always contingent, always subject to renegotiation, always vulnerable to the claim that a particular obligation violates a particular right. The Singaporean formulation, in LKY's version, began from the opposite direction. The survival of the collective was the starting point; the rights of the individual were derived from the collective's need for competent, cooperative, committed citizens. Rights were not pre-political. They did not exist in a state of nature, waiting to be protected by a social contract. They were created by the state, guaranteed by the state, and contingent on the state's continued existence, which was itself contingent on the willingness of citizens to meet their obligations. The citizen who refused national service, who evaded taxes, who treated public property as disposable, who demanded rights while disclaiming responsibilities, was not exercising a legitimate political preference. He was violating the deal, and a society that permitted widespread violation of the deal was a society that had made its own destruction a matter of time.

This framing was offensive to Western sensibilities, and it was intended to be. LKY was not trying to persuade Western liberals that his social contract was more philosophically defensible than theirs. He was pointing out that their social contract rested on a foundation they had not built and could not replicate. The Western rights-first tradition emerged from societies that had enjoyed centuries of internal stability, external security, and economic growth — conditions that made it possible to treat the individual's relationship to the state as a matter of philosophical refinement rather than survival necessity. A society that had not been invaded for two hundred years, whose borders were secured by geography or alliance, whose economy was large enough to absorb the costs of widespread nonperformance of civic obligations, could afford to debate whether mandatory national service violated individual autonomy. A society that had been invaded three years before its founding, whose water supply depended on a treaty with a larger neighbor, whose GDP was smaller than a midsized American city's municipal budget, could not. The rights-first framework was not wrong. It was contingent, and the contingency was the product of conditions that the framework's advocates had not created and could not guarantee. To export the framework to societies that did not share the conditions, or to maintain it in societies where the conditions were deteriorating, was not to uphold universal principles. It was to mistake historical luck for philosophical necessity, and the mistake, as LKY never tired of pointing out, was one that countries without Singapore's constraints would discover the cost of only when the conditions that had made it possible no longer obtained.

The question the deal poses is not whether Singapore's version is universally applicable. It is not. It was designed for a specific set of conditions, and its applicability to other societies depends on whether those societies face conditions that make its terms necessary. The question is whether the Western version is sustainable under the conditions the twenty-first century is imposing: declining economic growth, rising public debt, demographic contraction, intensifying geopolitical competition, and the arrival of technologies that will make the distinction between internal stability and external threat increasingly difficult to maintain. A social contract that assumes perpetual peace, perpetual prosperity, and perpetual individual choice is a social contract designed for a world that is ending. The deal LKY offered was designed for a world that was always precarious, and its terms — explicit, enforced, non-negotiable — are the terms that precarious worlds require.
