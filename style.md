# Voice & Style Specification

Write in the style of a historian who is also a polemicist — someone who respects the reader's intelligence but never forgets they're making an argument, not compiling a reference. The prose is clean, direct, and propulsive. Paragraphs earn their place or get cut. Anecdotes appear only when they carry real evidential weight — never as decoration. The tone is confident without being smug, willing to state uncomfortable conclusions plainly without hedging or preemptive apology. Technical and economic concepts are explained through concrete examples rather than jargon. The authorial stance is that of someone who has spent real time in the places being discussed — not a tourist, not a theorist. Humor is dry and infrequent, deployed for precision rather than warmth. The reader should feel they are being trusted with a frank assessment by someone who has done the work and is not interested in flattering anyone — not the East, not the West, not the reader. Think Niall Ferguson's argumentative clarity crossed with Lee Kuan Yew's brutal directness, written at the pace and accessibility of a strong Atlantic longread.

## Anti-Patterns

- No throat-clearing. No "In this chapter, we will explore..." No "It is important to note that..."
- No hedging stacks. One qualification per claim maximum. If you need three hedges, the claim isn't ready.
- No false balance. Present the strongest version of each position, then make the argument. Don't pretend all views are equal when they aren't.
- No anecdote padding. If a story doesn't carry evidential weight, cut it. Gladwell uses anecdotes to entertain; this book uses them to prove.
- No jargon without payoff. Every technical term must earn its place by enabling a point that plain language can't make as precisely.

## Structural Rules

- Open chapters with a concrete scene, image, or data point — never with abstraction.
- Close chapters with a forward vector — the reader should feel pulled into the next chapter, not released.
- Subchapters should function as self-contained arguments that build on each other, not as topic buckets.
- Vary paragraph length. Dense argument needs short paragraphs. Narrative passages can breathe.
- Use data as ammunition, not decoration. Every statistic must serve the argument. No stat-dumping.
